A non-disclosure agreement (NDA) is a contract that protects confidential information shared between two parties. For freelancers, NDAs come up in two common situations: a client wants you to sign one before sharing sensitive business details, or you want one in place before pitching a new idea or sharing your own proprietary process. This generator creates a clean, professional NDA in minutes — tailored to your situation.

Mutual vs one-way NDA: which one do you need?

A mutual NDA binds both parties to confidentiality. If you and a client are sharing sensitive information in both directions — discussing business strategy, exploring a new product concept, or collaborating on something that requires openness on both sides — a mutual NDA is the right choice.

A one-way NDA (also called a unilateral NDA) only protects one side. If a client is sharing proprietary business information with you — say, their internal processes, unreleased product roadmap, or financial data — but you're not sharing anything sensitive in return, a one-way NDA where the client is the disclosing party makes more sense.

What a freelance NDA should cover

The definition of confidential information is the most important clause. It should be specific enough to be enforceable but broad enough to actually protect what matters. Vague definitions like "any information shared" are often unenforceable. Specific descriptions — client lists, financial projections, unreleased product specifications, proprietary workflows — are more defensible.

The duration of the NDA matters too. Most freelance NDAs run one to three years. Indefinite NDAs are harder to enforce and may signal overcaution from a client. Two years is a reasonable standard for most freelance engagements.

NDA vs freelance contract: you often need both

An NDA only covers confidentiality. It doesn't address scope of work, payment terms, intellectual property, or what happens if a project gets cancelled. Most professional freelance engagements need both a signed NDA and a full project contract. Sign the NDA first — before any sensitive information changes hands — then use our Freelance Contract Generator to cover the full project terms before work begins.

Getting an NDA signed quickly

Use an e-signature tool like DocuSign or HelloSign to send the PDF for signature without back-and-forth. The key is timing: get the NDA signed before you discuss anything the other party would want protected. Once information has been shared, an NDA signed afterward doesn't cover it retroactively.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an NDA and when do freelancers need one? +

An NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement) is a contract that prohibits one or both parties from sharing confidential information. Freelancers typically need NDAs when working with proprietary business information, trade secrets, unreleased products, or sensitive client data.

Should I ask the client to sign an NDA, or will they send one? +

Larger companies often have their own NDA templates they'll ask you to sign. For smaller clients or when you're sharing your own proprietary methods, you can initiate with your own NDA. This generator creates a straightforward mutual or one-way NDA.

Is an NDA legally enforceable? +

Yes, if it's properly drafted and covers reasonable scope. Overly broad NDAs (e.g., trying to cover all publicly available information) may not hold up in court. The templates here are designed to be enforceable and reasonable.

What's the difference between a mutual and one-way NDA? +

A one-way NDA protects information flowing in one direction — typically from client to freelancer. A mutual NDA protects both parties. Use mutual NDAs when both sides are sharing sensitive information.

How long should an NDA last? +

Most freelance NDAs run for 1–3 years. Perpetual NDAs (no expiration) are often unenforceable or unreasonable. Two years after project completion is a common and enforceable standard.

Can an NDA prevent me from working with similar clients? +

A standard NDA shouldn't restrict future work. What you want to avoid is a non-compete clause buried in an NDA — these are different and can restrict who you work with. If you see non-compete language, negotiate or remove it before signing.
