New Client Welcome Email Template

The moment a client says yes is when expectations start forming. A welcome email sent within hours of that confirmation sets a professional tone, prevents confusion about next steps, and reduces the back-and-forth that slows projects down before they even start.

When to send Send within a few hours of the client confirming they want to proceed — ideally the same day. Don't let the momentum cool before you've established the next steps.

The Template

Email Template
Subject: Welcome Aboard - Next Steps for [Project Name]
Hi [Client Name], I'm thrilled to be working with you on [project name]. I wanted to send a quick welcome note and outline what happens next so we're both on the same page. Here's what to expect: 1. Contract: I'll send the project contract within [X hours/1 business day] for your review and signature. 2. Intake: I'll follow up with a short questionnaire to make sure I have everything I need to deliver great work. 3. Kickoff: Once the contract is signed and the deposit is received, we'll [schedule a kickoff call / I'll get started on [date]]. Project timeline: [start date] to [estimated completion date] Payment: [deposit amount] upfront, remainder due [upon completion / at milestone X] If you have any questions in the meantime, don't hesitate to reach out. I'm looking forward to this. Best, [Your Name]

Tips for Getting This Right

  • Number your next steps so the client knows exactly what to expect and in what order.
  • Confirm the payment terms explicitly here — stating them early prevents awkward conversations when the invoice arrives.
  • Include the deposit requirement if you have one. State it as a standard part of your process, not an afterthought.
  • Give a rough project timeline even if it's approximate — clients want to know when to expect things to start and end.
  • Keep the tone warm but organized. You're signaling that you run a professional operation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Waiting too long to send it — a welcome email sent two days later feels disorganized and lets uncertainty set in.
  • Skipping it entirely and jumping straight into work — without this email, the client doesn't know what to expect and small gaps turn into frustration.
  • Making it too long — this is a quick orientation, not a project brief. Save the detail for the contract and kickoff call.
  • Not confirming the deposit requirement — clients shouldn't be surprised by this when the invoice arrives.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I send a contract at the same time as the welcome email?

You can, but it's often cleaner to send them separately — welcome email first to set the tone, then the contract within a few hours or the next business day. Sending both at once can feel overwhelming.

What if I don't require a deposit?

Skip that part of the template. Not all freelancers require deposits — it depends on the project size, the client relationship, and your personal policy. But for new clients on larger projects, deposits are strongly recommended.

What should I include in the client intake questionnaire?

Project goals, target audience, key competitors, brand guidelines, existing assets, timeline preferences, and communication preferences. The goal is to get everything you need to start before the first working session.

How formal should this email be?

Match the tone of how you've been communicating with the client. If your conversations have been casual, a slightly warm tone is fine. If it's been formal, keep it professional. Consistency matters more than a specific register.
