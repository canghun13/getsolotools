Asking for a Referral Email Template

Referrals convert at a dramatically higher rate than any other source of freelance clients. They come pre-sold, pre-trusted, and pre-qualified. And yet most freelancers never ask for them, either because they forget or because it feels awkward. It doesn't have to.

When to send Send this 1-2 weeks after a successful project completion, while the experience is fresh. The longer you wait, the less likely the client is to be thinking about you.

The Template

Email Template
Subject: A Small Ask - [Your Name]
Hi [Client Name], I hope you're doing well. I really enjoyed working on [project name] with you and I'm glad it came together well. I'm currently looking to take on new projects, and I wondered if you might know anyone in your network who could use help with [your service — be specific]. Even a brief email introduction would mean a lot. Of course, absolutely no pressure — I just wanted to ask. Either way, it was a pleasure working with you and I'd love to work together again when the next opportunity comes up. Best, [Your Name]

Tips for Getting This Right

  • Be specific about the type of work or client you're looking for — 'anyone who needs help' is too vague for a client to act on.
  • Make it easy to say no — 'no pressure at all' language paradoxically makes people more likely to help.
  • Separate this email from the completion email — sending a referral request at the same time as the final invoice feels transactional.
  • Keep it short. A referral request should be 3-4 sentences, not a paragraph explaining your services.
  • If a client refers someone to you, always follow up to thank them — it reinforces the behavior and keeps the relationship warm.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Asking before the project is complete — wait until the client is happy with your work before asking for a favor.
  • Asking clients who weren't happy with the project — a lukewarm referral can do more harm than good.
  • Not following up when someone does refer you — failing to acknowledge a referral is one of the fastest ways to burn a relationship.
  • Asking too broadly — 'anyone who needs a freelancer' is unhelpful. Tell them exactly who you want to be introduced to.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I offer an incentive for referrals?

It's optional. A small gift card or a discount on future work can be a nice gesture, but many clients will refer you simply because they had a good experience. Offering money for referrals can sometimes make the relationship feel transactional.

What if I don't have any clients yet?

Ask former colleagues, professors, or people in your network instead. The referral request doesn't have to go to a paying client — anyone who knows your work and has relevant connections is a good candidate.

How often can I ask the same client for referrals?

Once per project, or once or twice a year for long-term clients. Any more than that becomes awkward.

What's the best way to make referrals easy for the client?

Give them a short summary they can forward — two or three sentences describing what you do and who you help. The easier you make it, the more likely they'll actually do it.
