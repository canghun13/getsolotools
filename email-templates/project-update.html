Project Status Update Email Template

Clients who feel uninformed become anxious clients who send check-in emails and schedule unnecessary calls. A brief, structured update sent at a predictable cadence eliminates all of that. The format matters as much as the content — scannable is better than thorough.

When to send Weekly on longer projects, or after each major milestone on shorter ones. Agree on the update frequency at kickoff so clients know when to expect it.

The Template

Email Template
Subject: Project Update: [Project Name] - [Date]
Hi [Client Name], Here's a quick update on [project name]. Completed: - [Task or deliverable completed] - [Task or deliverable completed] In progress: - [Current task] - on track for [date] Up next: - [Next milestone or deliverable] by [date] Blockers / Action needed: - [None at the moment] OR [I need [X] from you by [date] to stay on schedule] Overall status: On track for [final delivery date]. Let me know if you have any questions. [Your Name]

Tips for Getting This Right

  • Use the same structure every time — completed, in progress, up next, blockers. It becomes easy to scan and easy to write.
  • Always flag blockers explicitly and say what you need from the client and by when. Buried in prose, blockers get missed.
  • If you're behind, say so proactively. Clients handle bad news better when it's not a surprise.
  • Keep it under 200 words. A status update should take less than 60 seconds to read.
  • Send at the same time each week — predictability builds confidence.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Sending updates only when something goes wrong — by then it's a crisis, not an update.
  • Writing a wall of text — use bullet points and short sentences. Nobody reads paragraphs in a status email.
  • Burying blockers at the end — if you need something from the client, make it impossible to miss.
  • Skipping updates on short projects — even a one-week project benefits from a midpoint check-in.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should a project update email be?

As short as possible. Three to five bullet points covering what's done, what's in progress, and what's next is ideal. Add a blocker section only if there's something the client needs to act on.

What if the project is behind schedule?

Say so directly. State what's causing the delay, the new expected delivery date, and what you're doing to recover time if possible. Clients respect honesty — they don't respect finding out at the last minute.

Should I send updates even when there's nothing major to report?

Yes — a brief 'everything is on track, next update Friday' message is better than silence. Silence makes clients nervous even when things are fine.

What if the client never reads my updates?

Keep sending them anyway. They serve as a paper trail that protects you if there's ever a dispute about what was communicated and when.
