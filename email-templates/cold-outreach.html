Freelance Cold Outreach Email Template

Cold outreach gets a bad reputation because most of it is terrible — generic, self-focused, and obviously templated. The emails that actually work are the ones that feel like they were written for one specific person. That takes more effort, but the conversion rate is dramatically higher.

When to send Cold outreach works best when you can point to something specific and timely — a recent blog post they published, a product they just launched, a job listing that signals they need help. Timeliness makes your outreach relevant rather than random.

The Template

Email Template
Subject: [Something specific you noticed about their work] - [Your Name]
Hi [Name], I came across [their company / their work / a specific piece you noticed] and wanted to reach out. I'm a freelance [your role] who helps [type of client] with [specific outcome you deliver]. [One sentence about a relevant result or project — make it concrete.] I noticed [specific observation about their business, content, or project that relates to what you do] and thought there could be a good fit here. Would you be open to a 15-minute call to explore whether I can help? Best, [Your Name] [Portfolio or website link]

Tips for Getting This Right

  • Write the subject line last — it should reference something specific about them, not introduce you.
  • Lead with something you genuinely noticed about their work. It proves you're not mass-blasting and earns a few extra seconds of attention.
  • Keep the focus on them, not you. One sentence on your background is enough.
  • Ask for a 15-minute call, not a project. Low-commitment asks get more responses.
  • Follow up exactly once, 5-7 days later, if there's no response. After that, move on.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Starting with 'My name is [X] and I'm a freelance [Y]' — nobody cares about you yet. Start with them.
  • Writing more than 150 words — cold emails that are longer than a few sentences rarely get read to the end.
  • Sending a generic template to dozens of people — they can tell, and it tanks your response rate.
  • Attaching your portfolio unprompted — it comes across as assuming too much. Wait until they express interest.
  • Following up more than once — two unanswered emails is the limit. More than that crosses into harassment territory.

Variations

Responding to a job listing

Hi [Name], I saw your listing for [role/project type] and wanted to reach out before submitting a formal application. I'm a freelance [your role] who has done [relevant experience in one sentence]. [One concrete result or relevant project.] I'd love to learn more about the project before applying. Would you have 15 minutes for a quick call this week? [Your Name] [Portfolio link]

Frequently Asked Questions

What response rate should I expect from cold outreach?

A 5-10% response rate is considered good for well-targeted cold emails. If you're seeing less than 2-3%, the email or targeting needs work. Volume matters less than specificity.

Should I attach my portfolio?

Not in the first email. Include a link to your website or a relevant case study instead. An attachment in a cold email often triggers spam filters and feels presumptuous.

How do I find good prospects to cold email?

LinkedIn, company websites, job boards (as a signal of need, not a job application), industry directories, and people who engage with content in your niche. The best prospects are ones who clearly need what you offer right now.

Is cold email legal?

In the US, cold email to business addresses is generally permitted under CAN-SPAM rules as long as you include an unsubscribe option or respond promptly to opt-out requests. In the EU, GDPR applies stricter rules — avoid mass cold email to EU businesses without a legitimate interest basis.
