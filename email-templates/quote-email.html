Freelance Quote Email Template

A quote email should do one thing: give the client the number they asked for, with just enough context to understand what they're getting. Don't bury the price in paragraphs of preamble. Be clear, be direct, and make it easy for them to say yes — or to open a conversation if they want to negotiate.

When to send Send within 24 hours of a client asking for a quote. The faster you respond, the more professional you appear and the less likely they are to have moved on to another freelancer.

The Template

Email Template
Subject: Quote for [Project Name] - [Your Name/Business]
Hi [Client Name], Thanks for reaching out. Based on what you've shared about [project description], here's my quote: [Service or Deliverable 1]: $[amount] [Service or Deliverable 2]: $[amount] Total: $[total] This covers [brief scope summary]. The quote is valid for 14 days. Estimated timeline: [X weeks] from project start. Let me know if you have questions, or if you'd like to adjust the scope. I'm happy to hop on a quick call to discuss. Best, [Your Name]

Tips for Getting This Right

  • Break the quote down by deliverable or service — it shows transparency and helps the client understand where the cost comes from.
  • Set a validity period. A quote that never expires lets clients stall, then come back months later expecting the same price.
  • Include the timeline alongside the price — clients want to know when they'll have the work, not just what it costs.
  • Offer to adjust scope — it opens a conversation rather than forcing a binary yes/no decision.
  • Don't apologize for your rate. State it confidently.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Giving a range instead of a specific number — ranges make you look uncertain and clients always anchor to the lower end.
  • Under-quoting to win the project — you'll resent the work and the client will get lower quality than they'd have gotten if you'd priced it properly.
  • Not including a timeline — clients need to know when to expect delivery, not just how much it costs.
  • Forgetting to follow up — if you don't hear back within 3-5 days, a single follow-up is appropriate and often closes the deal.

Variations

Hourly rate quote

Hi [Client Name], Based on what you've described, here's my estimate: Rate: $[hourly rate]/hour Estimated hours: [X-Y hours] Estimated total: $[low]-$[high] I'll track time carefully and send a detailed breakdown with each invoice. The estimate is based on [scope assumptions]. Timeline: approximately [X weeks] from start. Let me know if you'd like to proceed or have questions. [Your Name]

Frequently Asked Questions

Fixed price or hourly — which should I quote?

Fixed price works best when the scope is clearly defined. Hourly is better for open-ended work or projects that are likely to evolve. Many freelancers use fixed price for most projects and hourly for revisions or ongoing work.

What if the client says my quote is too high?

Ask what budget they have in mind, then work backwards to see if you can reduce scope to fit it — not reduce your rate. If you can't make it work at a rate that's fair to you, it's okay to decline.

Should I include taxes in my quote?

In the US, freelancers generally don't charge sales tax on services (though this varies by state). Your quote should be your fee. Self-employment taxes are your own responsibility, not added to the client invoice.

How detailed should the scope description be in the quote email?

One or two sentences is enough for the email. The full scope detail goes in the contract or proposal. The quote email is just the number and a brief summary — save the detailed breakdown for later.
