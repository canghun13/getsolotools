Turning Down a Freelance Project Email Template

Turning down work feels counterintuitive, especially early in your freelance career. But saying yes to the wrong project — wrong timeline, wrong budget, wrong client — costs you far more than the project is worth. A professional decline keeps the door open, protects your reputation, and sometimes even leads to a referral or a better engagement later.

When to send As soon as you've decided you can't or don't want to take the project. Don't leave the client waiting — a timely no is more professional than a delayed yes followed by problems.

The Template

Email Template
Subject: Re: [Project Name] — Unfortunately Can't Take This On
Hi [Client Name], Thank you for reaching out about [project name] — it sounds like a great project. Unfortunately, I'm not able to take this on right now due to [brief reason: my current workload / a prior commitment / the timeline not fitting my availability]. If you're open to a later start date, I'd be available from [date] and would love to discuss it then. Alternatively, I'm happy to recommend a few colleagues who do excellent work in this area if that would be helpful. I hope we get the chance to work together in the future — please don't hesitate to reach out again. Best, [Your Name]

Tips for Getting This Right

  • Respond quickly — leaving someone waiting for an answer is worse than a prompt no.
  • Give a reason, but keep it brief. You don't owe a detailed explanation, but a one-line reason is courteous.
  • Offer an alternative where possible — a later date, a recommendation, or a resource. It makes a 'no' into something more useful.
  • Keep the door open for future work — freelancing is a relationship business and circumstances change.
  • Don't apologise excessively — one 'unfortunately' is enough. Over-apologising makes a simple business decision feel like a personal failing.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Ghosting the inquiry — not responding is unprofessional and damages your reputation even if you never worked with the person.
  • Being vague about whether you're declining — 'let me think about it' when you've already decided no wastes everyone's time.
  • Over-explaining or making up excuses — a simple, honest reason is always better than a fabricated one.
  • Declining rudely or citing the client as the reason — even if the client is the problem, keep it professional.

Variations

Declining due to budget mismatch

Hi [Client Name], Thank you for reaching out about [project name]. After reviewing the scope and budget, I don't think I'm the right fit for this one — the budget is below my minimum for a project of this scale. I wouldn't want to take it on and not be able to give it the attention it deserves. If your budget has flexibility, I'm happy to revisit. Otherwise, I'd be glad to point you toward some talented freelancers who may be a better fit at this price point. Best, [Your Name]

Declining due to wrong fit / outside expertise

Hi [Client Name], Thank you for thinking of me for [project name] — it sounds like an exciting project. Honestly, [specific aspect] is a bit outside my core expertise, and I wouldn't be confident delivering the quality you need. I'd rather be upfront about that than take the project and underdeliver. [Optional: I can recommend [Freelancer Name] who specialises in this area and does excellent work.] I hope you find the right person for it, and please keep me in mind for anything in [your core area]. Best, [Your Name]

Frequently Asked Questions

Will turning down work hurt my reputation?

No — a professional, timely decline actually builds reputation. It signals that you're selective, busy, and honest about what you can deliver. What hurts reputation is accepting work you can't deliver, ghosting inquiries, or being rude when declining.

Should I recommend another freelancer when I decline?

If you genuinely know someone who'd be a good fit, yes. It's a great way to add value even in a 'no' and often leads to reciprocal referrals. Don't recommend someone just to fill the gap if you're not confident in their work — your reputation is attached to the referral.

What if I want to decline but might want to work with this client later?

Be explicit about keeping the door open — say you'd love to work together in the future and mention when you might have availability. Following up proactively a few months later is a professional touch that very few freelancers do.

Is it okay to decline without giving a reason?

Technically yes — you don't owe an explanation. But a brief, professional reason is courteous and more likely to preserve the relationship. 'Workload' or 'prior commitments' is always acceptable and rarely questioned.
