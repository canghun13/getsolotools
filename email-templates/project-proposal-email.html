Project Proposal Email Template

The email you send with your proposal is the first thing the client reads — before they open a single page of the document itself. It needs to be warm, confident, and concise. Your job here is to make the client want to open the attachment, not to put everything in the email.

When to send Send within 24-48 hours of your discovery call or initial conversation. Longer than that and the momentum fades. If you need more time to put together a thorough proposal, send a brief email acknowledging the conversation and setting an expectation for when the proposal will arrive.

The Template

Email Template
Subject: Project Proposal: [Project Name] - [Your Name]
Hi [Client Name], Thank you for taking the time to discuss [project name] with me. I've put together a proposal based on our conversation and I'm genuinely excited about the opportunity to work together. The proposal covers: - Project scope and deliverables - Proposed timeline - Investment and payment terms - Next steps Please find the full proposal attached. I'm happy to walk you through it on a call or answer any questions via email — whichever works better for you. I've set a validity period of 14 days on this proposal. If you'd like to move forward or have any questions, please get in touch by [date]. Looking forward to hearing from you. Best, [Your Name]

Tips for Getting This Right

  • Briefly list what's in the proposal so the client knows what to expect — it lowers the barrier to opening it.
  • Use the word 'investment' instead of 'cost' or 'price' — it subtly frames your services as value-generating rather than a line-item expense.
  • Set a validity date — it creates urgency and prevents clients from sitting on proposals indefinitely.
  • Offer a call to walk them through it. Many clients won't read every word of a proposal — a brief call dramatically increases your close rate.
  • Personalize the opening line. Reference something specific from your conversation to show this isn't a template.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Sending the proposal without following up — a proposal sent with no follow-up has a much lower close rate than one followed up within 3-5 days.
  • Writing a novel in the email itself — the proposal is the document. The email just introduces it.
  • Not setting a validity period — open-ended proposals let clients delay indefinitely or come back months later expecting the same price.
  • Sending too late — proposals sent more than 48 hours after a conversation arrive when the client's enthusiasm has cooled.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should a freelance proposal be?

Long enough to answer every question the client has, short enough that they'll actually read it. For most projects, 2-5 pages is ideal. Include scope, timeline, pricing, and terms. Skip filler content about your company history unless specifically relevant.

Should I include pricing in the proposal email or only in the document?

Keep pricing in the document, not the email. The email is an introduction — you want the client to open the proposal and read through the full scope before they see the price.

What if the client wants changes to the proposal?

That's a normal part of the process. Treat it as a negotiation, not a rejection. Make adjustments, re-send, and set a new validity period on the revised version.

Should I follow up if I don't hear back?

Yes — send one follow-up 3-5 business days after sending the proposal. One follow-up is professional. Multiple follow-ups become annoying. Use the proposal follow-up template for this.
