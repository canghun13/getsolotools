How to Send a Freelance Quote That Wins the Project

June 6, 2026 · 7 min read

A freelance quote is often the first formal document a prospective client sees from you. Before they've reviewed your contract, before you've invoiced them, before any work begins — the quote is your first chance to show that you're professional, clear, and worth what you're charging. A well-written quote doesn't just communicate a price; it frames the project, shows you understood what the client needs, and reduces the back-and-forth that slows projects down.

Quote vs proposal: what's the difference?

These terms get used interchangeably, but they serve different purposes. A quote is a price document — it states what you'll deliver and what it will cost, usually in a fairly structured format. A proposal is a more complete sales document — it includes context about the problem, your approach, why you're the right person, case studies or relevant experience, and then pricing.

For smaller, clearly defined projects, a quote is usually all you need. For larger engagements or when competing against other freelancers, a full proposal makes more sense. This guide focuses on quotes — the day-to-day pricing documents most freelancers send regularly.

What a freelance quote should include

Your information and the client's information

Basic but easy to forget. Your name or business name, your contact information, and the client's name and company. Date the quote was issued and give it a quote number — this makes it easy to reference later if the client has questions or if you need to follow up.

A clear description of what's included

This is the most important part of the quote and where most freelancers are too vague. "Website design" tells a client almost nothing. "Design and development of a 5-page website including Home, About, Services, Contact, and Blog — responsive, optimized for mobile, delivered as WordPress with client-editable content" tells them exactly what they're getting and sets expectations before work begins.

Be specific about deliverables, not just the category of work. Specificity also protects you — a vague scope leads to scope creep, where clients reasonably assume that related work is included when it wasn't in your pricing.

What's not included

This is optional but valuable for projects where clients might assume related services are bundled in. A web designer might note: "Does not include copywriting, stock photography licensing, or ongoing maintenance." A photographer might note: "Does not include hair and makeup, location permits, or printing." Spelling out exclusions prevents misunderstandings that are awkward to resolve after the fact.

Pricing

State the price clearly. If it's a fixed fee, show the total. If you're billing hourly, show your rate and estimated hours. If there are multiple options or tiers, present them clearly. Don't make the client calculate totals or guess what they're actually paying.

If your quote includes optional add-ons — additional pages, rush delivery, extended licensing — list them separately with their prices so the client can make an informed choice.

Payment terms

Include your payment structure (50% upfront, 50% on delivery is standard for most project-based work), accepted payment methods, and when payment is due. Include a brief note about your late fee policy — not as a threat, but as a standard business practice. Clients who plan to pay on time won't blink; clients who planned to drag their feet will know you take payment seriously.

Quote validity period

A quote should have an expiration date — 30 days is standard. This prevents a client from accepting a quote months later at pricing that no longer reflects your rates or availability. It also creates a soft deadline that encourages a decision.

How to handle rush requests: If a client needs work faster than your standard timeline allows, it's reasonable to charge a rush premium — typically 20–50% above your standard rate depending on how compressed the schedule is. Note this explicitly in the quote so there's no ambiguity about why the price is higher than they expected.

How to price a quote accurately

Pricing is where most freelancers either leave money on the table or lose projects by coming in too high. A few principles that help:

Estimate time honestly, then add a buffer. Most freelancers underestimate how long projects take because they forget to account for client feedback rounds, revisions, admin time, and the cognitive overhead of context-switching between projects. A realistic estimate includes all of that, not just the direct execution time. Adding 20% to your time estimate as a buffer is a reasonable starting point.

Factor in your actual costs. If a project requires you to purchase stock assets, license fonts, pay for specialized software, or bring in subcontractors, those costs belong in the quote — either billed back to the client directly or factored into your fee. Don't absorb project-specific costs as overhead.

Price for the value, not just the time. A logo that a client will use for ten years on every piece of marketing material is worth more than the hours you spent designing it. Pricing based purely on time often undervalues work that has significant long-term impact. This is a bigger conversation than a single quote, but it's worth keeping in mind when you're deciding whether to price based on hours or on the scope and value of the deliverable.

Common quoting mistakes that cost freelancers money

Quoting before you understand the project

Don't send a quote based on a vague brief. If you don't have enough information to scope the project accurately, ask questions first. A discovery call or a written brief questionnaire takes 30 minutes and saves you from quoting a price that doesn't match what the client actually needs — which leads to either doing more work than you priced, or having an uncomfortable conversation mid-project about additional costs.

Not following up

Most clients don't respond to quotes immediately, even when they're interested. A follow-up three to five days after sending the quote is professional and expected. Something as simple as "Wanted to check in on the quote I sent — happy to answer any questions or adjust the scope if needed" is enough. Many projects are won at the follow-up stage, not the initial quote.

Caving immediately on price

If a client asks for a discount, don't immediately agree. Ask what's driving the budget constraint — sometimes it's a legitimate budget ceiling, sometimes it's a negotiating habit. If you do reduce your price, reduce your scope proportionally. A lower price for the same scope signals that your original quote wasn't based on real value, which sets a difficult precedent for the working relationship.

Quote to contract: what happens next

A quote is not a contract. Once a client accepts your quote, the next step is a signed project contract that covers everything in more detail — scope, payment schedule, revision policy, IP ownership, and what happens if either party needs to end the engagement early. Don't start work based on a quote acceptance alone.

The sequence that protects you: quote accepted → contract signed → deposit received → work begins. Skipping any of those steps introduces risk. Our Contract Generator makes it straightforward to create a full project contract once your quote is approved.

Sending the quote

Send it as a PDF — not an editable document. A PDF looks more professional, prevents accidental editing, and ensures it displays consistently across devices. Include a short email that references the quote, thanks the client for the conversation, and makes clear what the next step is: "If you'd like to move forward, let me know and I'll send a contract for review."

Keep a copy of every quote you send in a client folder alongside correspondence, the signed contract, and all invoices. If there's ever a disagreement about what was agreed to, having organized documentation is invaluable.
