Pennsylvania's Legal Interest Rate

Under Pennsylvania law (41 P.S. § 201), the legal rate of interest is 6% per year when no rate is specified in an agreement. That translates to 0.5% per month — well below what most freelancers charge. For commercial contracts between businesses, Pennsylvania allows parties to agree to a higher rate in writing, and courts routinely enforce commercial late fee clauses well above 6%.

The practical standard for Pennsylvania freelancers is 1.5% per month (18% annually) — the same rate used in most other commercial contexts. At this rate, a $3,000 invoice that goes 60 days overdue accumulates $90 in late fees. Use our Late Payment Fee Calculator to see the exact total for your situation. This rate is clearly enforceable with a written contract specifying it upfront.

Consumer vs. commercial distinction: Pennsylvania's 6% cap applies to consumer transactions and situations where no rate is specified. For business-to-business freelance engagements — which most freelance work qualifies as — the parties can contractually set a higher rate. The key is having it in writing before work begins.

The Philadelphia Wage Theft Law

Philadelphia passed a Wage Theft ordinance that extends beyond employees to cover some independent contractors. If you perform work within Philadelphia city limits for a client based in Philadelphia, and you go unpaid, you may be able to file a complaint with the Philadelphia Office of Labor and your city's wage theft enforcement unit.

This is meaningful because wage theft enforcement gives you a government agency as an ally in collecting — not just your own legal options. Enforcement is not guaranteed and the ordinance has limits (it focuses on wage theft patterns, not every delayed invoice), but it's a resource that freelancers in Philadelphia should know exists.

The practical threshold: if a Philadelphia client simply refuses to pay for completed work, or systematically delays payment, a wage theft complaint adds pressure that a standard collection letter does not. It also costs nothing to file.

Pittsburgh and other PA cities: Pittsburgh and other Pennsylvania municipalities do not have equivalent freelancer-specific wage theft ordinances covering independent contractors at this time. Outside Philadelphia, your options are contract enforcement and civil court.

Pennsylvania's Prompt Payment Act for Government Work

Pennsylvania's Prompt Payment Act (62 Pa. C.S. § 3931 et seq.) applies to state government contracts. If you perform work for a Pennsylvania state agency, payment is due within 45 days of a proper invoice. If payment is late, interest accrues at the rate specified in your contract, or at the prevailing interest rate set by the State Treasurer if no rate is specified.

For local government and municipal clients, payment terms and interest on late payment are governed by the specific contract. Always review payment terms carefully before starting any government work in Pennsylvania, as the statutory protections vary significantly by agency and contract type.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in Pennsylvania

Pennsylvania courts will enforce a written late fee clause that was clearly disclosed before the contract was signed. To make your late fee clause stick:

  • State the rate explicitly. "A late fee of 1.5% per month will be charged on invoices unpaid after 30 days from the invoice date" is enforceable. Vague language like "late fees may apply" is not.
  • Include it in your contract, not just your invoice. If you send a late fee clause only on the invoice but not in the signed agreement, a court may find it wasn't properly agreed to.
  • Repeat it on every invoice. State the due date and late fee policy on every invoice you send. Accounts payable staff often process invoices without reference to the underlying contract.
  • Send a written notice when fees begin accruing. An email confirming the overdue balance and that late fees are now running is both a professional follow-up and a paper trail that helps in court.
  • Issue an updated invoice. When following up on an overdue balance, send a new invoice showing the original amount, fees accrued to date, and the new total. Keep the tone professional — you want payment, not a fight.

Edge Case: What Happens When a Client Disputes the Work?

Pennsylvania courts distinguish between clients who fail to pay and clients who dispute whether the work was completed or acceptable. If a client raises a good-faith dispute about deliverable quality, a court may reduce or eliminate late fees on the disputed portion, even if your contract includes a clear late fee clause.

The practical defense against this: document delivery. Send completed work by email with a clear subject line indicating what's being delivered. Get written client approval at each milestone. If a client approves work in writing and then later disputes it to avoid paying, you have evidence that contradicts the dispute. Approval emails are worth more than any late fee clause when a dispute reaches court.

Collecting in Pennsylvania

Pennsylvania Magisterial District Courts handle small claims up to $12,000 without requiring a lawyer. These courts are designed for straightforward disputes and are accessible to freelancers. Filing fees are modest, and cases are typically heard within 30 to 60 days.

For amounts between $12,000 and $35,000, the Court of Common Pleas handles civil cases through a simplified arbitration process. Above $35,000, you're in full civil litigation and legal representation becomes important.

  • Magisterial District Court: up to $12,000 — no attorney required
  • Court of Common Pleas (compulsory arbitration): $12,000–$35,000
  • Court of Common Pleas (civil): over $35,000 — consider an attorney
  • Philadelphia Office of Labor: wage theft complaints for Philadelphia-based clients
  • Pennsylvania Attorney General: for patterns of fraud, not individual payment disputes

Practical Tips for Pennsylvania Freelancers

  • Pennsylvania's default interest rate is 6% annually — always specify a higher commercial rate in your contract
  • 1.5% per month (18% annually) is the widely accepted commercial standard and is enforceable with a written agreement
  • If you work with Philadelphia-based clients, know that the city's Wage Theft ordinance may give you an additional collection avenue
  • Government clients fall under the Pennsylvania Prompt Payment Act — review payment terms carefully before starting any state work
  • Document deliverable approvals in writing — a client's written approval destroys most good-faith dispute defenses
  • Magisterial District Court (up to $12,000) is fast, affordable, and doesn't require a lawyer

Pennsylvania's commercial contract framework gives freelancers solid ground to enforce late fees above the statutory default. The Philadelphia Wage Theft ordinance adds a layer of protection for freelancers in the city that most contractors don't know they have. Put your terms in writing, document your work, follow up promptly in writing when payment is late, and use the court system if it comes to that — Pennsylvania's small claims process is genuinely accessible.
