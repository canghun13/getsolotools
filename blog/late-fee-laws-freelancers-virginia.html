Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in Virginia

July 16, 2026 · 7 min read

Virginia has a quiet but useful protection most freelancers don't know about: if your invoice goes unpaid for more than 60 days and you never wrote a specific interest rate into your contract, Virginia law lets you charge interest automatically anyway. Here's what Virginia freelancers need to know about late fees, from the Code of Virginia's interest provisions to collecting through small claims court.

Virginia's 6% Legal Interest Rate — and an Automatic 60-Day Trigger

Virginia's legal interest rate is 6% per year (Va. Code § 6.2-301), and this is the rate courts apply to judgments and to contracts that don't specify their own rate (Va. Code § 6.2-302). What's less well known: under Va. Code § 6.2-302(C), if you sell goods or provide services on an open account — meaning there's no separate written credit agreement — and the client doesn't pay in full within 60 days of your invoice or billing statement, you're entitled to collect interest at the legal rate (6%) starting the day after that 60-day window closes. You don't need a late fee clause in your contract for this baseline protection to apply.

That's a helpful floor, but most Virginia freelancers still write the nationwide standard of 1.5% per month (18% annually) into their contracts, since it's both higher and enforceable as long as it's disclosed in writing before work begins.

Why Writing Your Own Rate Still Matters

Don't rely on the 60-day default alone: 6% annually is roughly a third of what most freelancers charge under a standard late fee clause. The automatic trigger is a useful backstop if you forgot to specify a rate on a particular project, but a written clause stating 1.5% per month gets you three times the compensation for the same overdue balance — and starts accruing sooner than 60 days if you set a shorter grace period.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in Virginia

  • Specify your own rate in writing. "Invoices unpaid after 15 days accrue interest at 1.5% per month" both beats the 60-day/6% default and starts the clock earlier.
  • Reference the rate on every invoice so it's visible without digging through the contract.
  • Keep it reasonable. 1.5–2% per month is standard and well within what Virginia courts expect.
  • Document your invoice dates. If you ever need to rely on the 60-day automatic rule, you'll need to show exactly when the invoice or billing statement was sent.

Collecting in Virginia

Virginia's General District Court handles small claims up to $5,000 through its small claims division — informal, no attorney required, and designed for straightforward money disputes like unpaid invoices. Claims above $5,000 still go to General District Court, just outside the small claims division, and can involve more formal procedure.

  • Virginia Small Claims (General District Court): up to $5,000 — no attorney required
  • Virginia General District Court (regular civil docket): for claims above $5,000, up to $25,000
  • Judgment interest: 6% annually or the contract rate, whichever is higher (Va. Code § 6.2-302)
