Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in Louisiana

July 20, 2026 · 7 min read

Louisiana runs on a different legal system than every other state — a civil-law tradition inherited from its French and Spanish history, not the common law that governs the other 49. That difference shows up directly in how late fees, interest rates, and even the word "statute of limitations" work. Here's what Louisiana freelancers need to know, from the 12% interest cap to a small claims process that gives up your right to appeal.

Louisiana's 12% Cap Is Lower Than the Rate Most Freelancers Charge

This is the detail that trips up freelancers who work with Louisiana clients using a template contract written for other states: under La. R.S. 9:3500(C), Louisiana's conventional interest rate — the rate parties agree to by contract — cannot exceed 12% per year, and it must be fixed in writing, since testimony alone can't prove a verbal interest agreement in court. The 1.5% monthly (18% annually) rate that's standard practically everywhere else in this series would exceed Louisiana's cap. The safe, enforceable equivalent in Louisiana is 1% per month (12% annually) — still a meaningful deterrent against late payment, just calibrated to the state's ceiling. Separately, judicial interest (the rate on money judgments after litigation) is set annually under La. R.S. 13:4202 and sits at 8.50% for 2026 — a different number entirely from your contract rate, and one that only applies after you've already gone to court.

Louisiana Doesn't Have a "Statute of Limitations" — It Has Prescription

Louisiana's civil-law roots mean it uses different terminology and, in one important way, different rules than the other 49 states. Instead of a "statute of limitations," Louisiana law calls the filing deadline prescription. For most freelance work, the relevant category is an open account — recurring or ongoing billing without a single formal written contract — which prescribes in just 3 years under Louisiana Civil Code article 3494. If you do have a single comprehensive written contract covering the whole engagement, that gets the longer 10-year period under article 3499 instead. The distinction matters: freelancers who invoice recurring clients under informal terms are working with the shorter 3-year clock, not the 10-year one, even if some communication was in writing.

Louisiana and Texas share a border but sit on opposite ends of nearly every metric here. Texas's Justice Courts allow claims up to $20,000 — four times Louisiana's $5,000 Small Claims Division cap — and Texas's 18% interest ceiling is exactly the standard rate most freelancers charge nationally, while Louisiana's 12% cap requires trimming that rate down. If you're a Louisiana-based freelancer who occasionally works with Texas clients (or vice versa), don't assume the same contract language and rate apply cleanly on both sides of the border; check which state's law governs the agreement before finalizing your terms.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in Louisiana

  • Cap your written rate at 12% annually (1% monthly). "Invoices unpaid after 15 days accrue interest at 1% per month" stays inside Louisiana's conventional interest ceiling.
  • Always get it in writing. Louisiana law explicitly bars testimonial proof of a verbal interest agreement above the legal rate — a text message or verbal "yes" isn't enough.
  • Know whether you're on an open account or a full written contract, since it changes your prescription period from 3 years to 10.
  • Decide between speed and appeal rights before filing. Small Claims Division is fast but final; the regular City Court docket takes longer but lets you appeal if you lose.

Louisiana Small Claims Division judgments cannot be appealed — at all. Under La. R.S. 13:5202, there is no appeal from a Small Claims Division judgment in Louisiana, a rule most other states don't share. That trade-off buys you speed and finality, but if your case is complex or the amount is near the $5,000 cap, filing in the regular City Court civil docket (which handles claims up to roughly $35,000) preserves your right to appeal at the cost of a longer process.

Collecting in Louisiana

Louisiana's Small Claims Division hears disputes up to $5,000 (La. R.S. 13:5200), with no appeal available once judgment is entered. Larger claims, or claims where you want to preserve appeal rights, go to the regular City Court civil docket.

  • Louisiana Small Claims Division: up to $5,000, no appeal permitted (La. R.S. 13:5200, 13:5202)
  • Louisiana City Court (regular civil docket): typically up to $35,000, appeal rights preserved
  • Prescription for open account claims: 3 years (La. Civ. Code art. 3494); written contracts: 10 years (art. 3499)
  • Judicial (post-judgment) interest rate: 8.50% for 2026, reset annually (La. R.S. 13:4202)
