Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in Nevada

Nevada's Floating Default Rate

Instead of a fixed percentage, Nevada's default interest rate under NRS 99.040(1) is tied to the prime rate at the largest bank in Nevada, plus 2%, recalculated every January 1 and July 1 until the debt is paid. As of 2026 that floating rate sits in the low double digits, but the exact figure shifts with the broader interest rate environment — which is exactly why most Nevada freelancers don't rely on it. Writing a flat, predictable rate like 1.5% per month (18% annually) directly into your contract and invoices avoids the guesswork entirely and is fully enforceable as the agreed rate, since NRS 99.040 only applies "when there is no express contract in writing fixing a different rate."

Collecting a Judgment Is a Separate Challenge in Nevada

Winning a case is one thing; collecting on it is another, and Nevada is more debtor-protective than most states on this front. Under NRS 21.090(1)(g), a debtor keeps the greater of 82% of disposable wages or 50 times the federal minimum wage — meaning wage garnishment can reach at most 18% of a nonpaying client's paycheck, compared to the 25% ceiling used as a baseline in most other states. Nevada judgments remain fully enforceable through bank account levies and property liens, so a win isn't hollow, but freelancers relying primarily on wage garnishment to collect should expect it to move more slowly here than elsewhere.

Nevada and Arizona share a border but differ sharply on small claims access: Nevada's $10,000 Justice Court limit is nearly three times Arizona's unusually low $3,500 cap, so a mid-sized unpaid invoice that requires Arizona's costlier Justice Court process can often stay in Nevada's cheaper small claims track. On rates, Arizona's 10% default is a fixed number freelancers can plan around, while Nevada's floating prime-plus-2% rate requires checking the current published figure — one more reason Nevada freelancers tend to specify their own flat rate in writing rather than rely on the statutory default.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in Nevada

  • Write a flat rate into every contract. "Invoices unpaid after 15 days accrue interest at 1.5% per month" avoids the floating statutory rate entirely and is easy for both sides to calculate.
  • Reference the rate on every invoice so the client sees it clearly, not just buried in the original agreement.
  • Keep it reasonable. 1.5–2% per month is standard nationally and well within what Nevada courts expect from a legitimate late fee.
  • File within 6 years. Nevada's statute of limitations for written contracts is 6 years (NRS 11.190(1)(b)), giving you a solid window to act.

Nevada bars assigned debt claims from small claims court. If you ever sell or assign an unpaid invoice to a collection agency, that agency generally cannot pursue it in Nevada's small claims court — one of only a handful of states with this consumer-protective rule. It doesn't affect freelancers collecting their own invoices directly, but it's worth knowing if you ever consider selling a debt.

Collecting in Nevada

Nevada Justice Court handles small claims disputes up to $10,000 (NRS 73.010), filed in the township where the defendant resides, does business, or is employed. Attorneys are permitted, unlike in some states, though most filers represent themselves.

  • Nevada Justice Court (small claims): up to $10,000
  • Written contract statute of limitations: 6 years (NRS 11.190(1)(b))
  • Post-judgment interest: prime rate plus 2%, reset every January 1 and July 1 (NRS 17.130)
  • Appeal window: just 5 judicial days from Justice Court to District Court — one of the shortest in the country (NRS 73.040)
