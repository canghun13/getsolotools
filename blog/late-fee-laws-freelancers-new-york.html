Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in New York

May 13, 2026 · Updated Jul 24, 2026 · 7 min read

New York is one of the strongest states in the country for freelancer protections — and since August 2024, that protection extends to the entire state, not just New York City. Between the statewide Freelance Isn't Free Act and general contract law, you have real legal tools to enforce late fees — but only if you set things up correctly from the start.

Here's what every New York freelancer needs to know about charging interest on overdue invoices, what the law says, and how to actually collect.

The Freelance Isn't Free Act: Now Statewide, Not Just NYC

If your late-paying client is in New York, you have more legal leverage than a standard contract dispute — and this applies whether you're in Manhattan or Buffalo. New York City's original Freelance Isn't Free Act (FIFA) took effect in 2017, but on August 28, 2024, New York State extended nearly identical protections to freelancers statewide under a new Article 44-A of the General Business Law. If your contract was signed on or after that date, you're covered no matter where in New York you work.

  • Applies to contracts worth $800 or more — either a single contract or multiple contracts with the same client that add up to $800 within a 120-day period
  • Contracts at or above that threshold must be in writing, and the hiring party must keep it on file for 6 years
  • Payment is due by the date in the contract, or within 30 days of completing the work if no date is specified
  • Excludes sales representatives, attorneys, licensed medical professionals, and construction contractors
  • Clients cannot retaliate against freelancers who assert their rights under the Act
  • Winning a non-payment claim gets you the amount owed plus double damages, attorney's fees, and injunctive relief — courts can also fine repeat-offender clients up to $25,000

Important — this replaces outdated advice you may have seen: Some older guides (including an earlier version of this page) said freelancers outside New York City weren't covered by FIFA and only had ordinary contract-law remedies. That's no longer accurate. Since August 28, 2024, the state law gives freelancers anywhere in New York the same double-damages protection NYC freelancers have had since 2017. If you're inside NYC specifically, you can still also use the original city law — enforced by the NYC Department of Consumer and Worker Protection — as an additional, parallel path.

You have six years to sue for a FIFA violation, and you can pursue it either as a civil lawsuit or by filing a complaint with the New York State Department of Labor or Attorney General. Neither route requires a lawyer to get started.

What Late Fee Rate Can You Charge in New York?

New York State law caps interest on consumer loans at 16% per year under civil usury law. However, late fees on business invoices are governed by contract — meaning if your client is a business (not an individual consumer), you can generally charge whatever rate you both agreed to in writing.

Most New York freelancers charge between 1.5% per month (18% annually) and 2% per month (24% annually). These rates are standard, defensible, and unlikely to raise objections from clients who understand how business works.

If you're invoicing individual consumers rather than businesses, it's safer to stay at or below 16% annually to avoid any usury issues.

How to Make Late Fees Enforceable

The most common reason freelancers can't collect late fees isn't legal — it's procedural. They never put it in writing. To make your late fees legally enforceable in New York:

  • State it in your contract. Include a clause like: "Invoices unpaid after [X] days will accrue interest at 1.5% per month on the outstanding balance."
  • Put it on your invoice. Add your payment terms to every invoice, including the due date and late fee rate.
  • Send a reminder before applying fees. A written reminder creates a paper trail and gives the client a chance to pay without dispute.
  • Send an updated invoice. Once fees kick in, send a new invoice showing the original amount, the fee, and the new total.

Grace Periods: Do You Need One?

You're not required to offer a grace period, but most freelancers build in 5–10 days before fees start accruing. This reduces friction with clients who are slow but not malicious, and makes you look reasonable if a dispute ever goes to court or small claims.

Whatever you decide, put it in writing. "Fees begin accruing 7 days after the due date" is clear and defensible.

Small Claims Court in New York

New York Small Claims Court handles disputes up to $10,000 in NYC Civil Court, and up to $5,000 in other parts of the state. It's designed to be accessible without a lawyer. If a client refuses to pay after multiple attempts:

  • File a claim at your local small claims court (usually costs $15–$20 to file)
  • Bring all documentation: the contract, invoices, emails, and any payment records
  • NYC freelancers can also file through the Freelance Isn't Free complaint process, which may result in faster resolution

Practical Tips for New York Freelancers

  • Always use a written contract — legally required statewide for projects over $800
  • Send invoices promptly after completing work to start the payment clock
  • Follow up in writing so you have a record of every communication
  • Don't wait 90+ days to escalate — the sooner you act, the better your odds of collecting
  • If a client disputes the fee, be willing to negotiate — half of what you're owed beats nothing

New York gives freelancers more tools than most states to enforce payment. Use them — but set things up correctly from day one so you have something to enforce.

What late fee rate is enforceable in New York? +

New York has no statutory late-fee cap for business-to-business invoices — the 16% civil usury cap applies to consumer loans, not commercial contracts. Most freelancers in New York charge 1.5% per month (18% annually), which is enforceable as long as it's clearly stated in your contract and on your invoice before work begins.

How does New York's small claims limit compare to nearby states? +

New York City's small claims court hears claims up to $10,000, but the limit drops to $5,000 in upstate city courts and just $3,000 in town and village courts outside the city. That's well above New Jersey's $3,000 statewide cap, but below Pennsylvania's $12,000 limit — so where in New York (and where a client is based) matters more than in most states.

Does the Freelance Isn't Free Act cover all of New York State now, or just New York City? +

As of August 28, 2024, it covers all of New York State. New York City's original 2017 law still applies within the five boroughs and is enforced separately by the NYC Department of Consumer and Worker Protection, but freelancers anywhere else in New York now get the same core protections — written contract requirement above $800, 30-day payment default, and double damages for non-payment — under the statewide law (Article 44-A of the General Business Law).
