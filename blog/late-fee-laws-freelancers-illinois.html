Here's exactly what the law covers, what late fees you can charge, and how to use it.

The Illinois Freelance Workers Protection Act

The Illinois Freelance Workers Protection Act (820 ILCS 252), effective July 1, 2024, applies to freelance contracts worth $500 or more — either as a single contract or as cumulative payments to one client over a 120-day period.

Under the Act, clients are required to pay freelancers by the date specified in the contract. If no date is specified, payment is due within 30 days of completing the work. Key provisions include:

  • Contracts over $500 must be in writing
  • Payment must arrive by the agreed date, or within 30 days of project completion if no date is set
  • Clients cannot retaliate against freelancers who assert their rights under the Act
  • Freelancers can file a complaint with the Illinois Department of Labor or pursue civil action
  • Violations can result in triple the unpaid amount plus attorney's fees

Important: The treble damages provision is a serious penalty for clients. Many freelancers have found that simply mentioning the Act — and your right to 3× damages — is enough to prompt payment from reluctant clients.

What Late Fee Rate Can You Charge in Illinois?

Illinois doesn't set a specific maximum late fee for commercial invoices. Courts apply a reasonableness standard: the fee must reflect actual harm caused by late payment, not act as a punishment.

The statutory interest rate in Illinois is 5% per year when no rate is specified in a contract. However, written contracts can override this — and most freelancers set their own rate in their agreements. The standard rate of 1.5% per month (18% annually) is widely used and considered reasonable. Some freelancers go up to 2% per month for clients with a history of late payment. How the Freelance Workers Protection Act Works in Practice

The Act gives you two enforcement paths: filing a complaint with the Illinois Department of Labor, or filing a civil lawsuit. Both are available to you, and you can pursue them even without an attorney.

If you win in court, you can recover:

  • The unpaid invoice amount
  • Up to twice the unpaid amount as additional damages (making it triple total)
  • Attorney's fees and court costs
  • Any other relief the court finds appropriate

This makes Illinois one of the stronger states for freelancer enforcement. The threat of triple damages shifts the risk to the client, which is exactly the point.

Making Your Late Fees Enforceable

The Act requires written contracts for projects over $500, but you should use written contracts for everything regardless of the amount. Here's what to include:

  • Payment terms. State the due date clearly, or specify "net 30 from project completion" if no fixed date applies.
  • Late fee clause. Something like: "Invoices unpaid after the due date will accrue interest at 1.5% per month on the outstanding balance."
  • Grace period (optional). Offering 5–7 days before fees kick in is good practice and reduces disputes.
  • Reference to the FWPA. You can include a note that the contract is subject to the Illinois Freelance Workers Protection Act. This signals to clients that you know your rights.

Tip: Put your payment terms on every invoice too — not just the contract. Clients often pass invoices to accounts payable, where the person processing the payment may never have seen your contract.

What If the Client Is Outside Illinois?

The Illinois FWPA applies if the freelancer lives or works within Illinois, or if the client is based in Illinois. If your client operates in another state, the Act may still apply depending on where the work was performed. When in doubt, include a governing law clause in your contract specifying that Illinois law applies.

Practical Tips for Illinois Freelancers

  • Use written contracts for every project — it's legally required over $500 and just good practice below that
  • State your late fee rate in both your contract and on every invoice
  • Send invoices promptly after completing work to start the payment clock
  • Follow up in writing — email is fine — so you have a timestamped record of your attempts to collect
  • If payment is late, cite the Illinois Freelance Workers Protection Act in your follow-up. Many clients will pay immediately rather than risk triple damages
  • For amounts under $10,000, Illinois Small Claims Court is an accessible option without a lawyer

Illinois freelancers have more tools than most to enforce payment. The FWPA is a genuine deterrent for clients who might otherwise string you along. Know your rights, document everything, and don't hesitate to use them.
