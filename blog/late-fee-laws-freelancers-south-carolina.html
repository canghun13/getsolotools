Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in South Carolina

South Carolina's Unusually Precise 8.75% Default Rate

Under S.C. Code § 34-31-20(A), South Carolina's legal interest rate on accounts stated and other ascertained debts is fixed at 8.75% per year — a specific figure rather than the round 6% or 8% most states default to. This is the rate that applies when there's no written agreement specifying interest, and it's noticeably higher than what most states charge by default, though still below the 1.5% monthly (18% annually) most freelancers write into their own contracts. Judgment interest works differently: under subsection (B), a money judgment draws interest at the prime rate plus 4 percentage points, compounded annually and reset by the state Supreme Court every January 15 — so a court judgment can actually earn more or less than the 8.75% pre-judgment rate depending on where prime sits that year.

South Carolina's Statute of Limitations Doesn't Play Favorites

South Carolina simplifies things in one respect: under S.C. Code § 15-3-530, a uniform 3-year statute of limitations applies to contract claims, property damage, and most other civil actions alike — no need to track separate deadlines depending on the type of dispute. That's shorter than many states' 4-to-6-year windows for written contracts, so freelancers who let an invoice sit too long risk losing the right to sue faster here than in most of the country. The clock starts running from the date the debt became due, not from when you first noticed the client had gone quiet.

South Carolina and North Carolina share the same 3-year statute of limitations, making them the most consistent pair on the East Coast for filing deadlines — a South Carolina freelancer moving a client relationship across the state line doesn't need to relearn the clock. Where they diverge is small claims access: North Carolina's $10,000 Magistrate Court limit is a third higher than South Carolina's $7,500, and Georgia's $15,000 cap is double. A South Carolina freelancer with a larger unpaid invoice may find it easier to stay in the no-lawyer-required small claims process by working with clients based just across either border.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in South Carolina

  • Write your rate into every contract. "Invoices unpaid after 15 days accrue interest at 1.5% per month" clears South Carolina's 8.75% default and is easy for both sides to calculate.
  • State the rate on the invoice itself, not just in the original agreement, so it's visible the moment payment becomes overdue.
  • Track the 3-year window carefully. Because South Carolina applies the same short deadline to nearly every civil claim, don't let an unpaid invoice sit longer than you would in a state with a longer statute of limitations.
  • Consider Magistrate Court early for invoices under $7,500 — it's designed for exactly this kind of straightforward money dispute.

South Carolina construction and prompt-payment work is different. If you do subcontracting or prime-contractor work covered by South Carolina's prompt payment statute, a separate 1% monthly interest rate applies automatically — but only if your invoice cites the statute and warns that interest may accrue. For general freelance services outside that narrow category, your contract's own rate (or the 8.75% default) governs instead.

Collecting in South Carolina

South Carolina's Magistrate Court handles small claims disputes up to $7,500 (S.C. Code § 22-3-10), filed in the county where the defendant lives or where the dispute occurred. Filing fees run around $80, plus roughly $40 for service.

  • South Carolina Magistrate Court (small claims): up to $7,500
  • Statute of limitations for written contracts: 3 years (S.C. Code § 15-3-530(1))
  • Pre-judgment legal interest rate: 8.75% annually (S.C. Code § 34-31-20(A))
  • Post-judgment interest: prime rate plus 4%, compounded annually, reset each January 15
