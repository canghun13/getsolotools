Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in Ohio

May 27, 2026 · Updated June 22, 2026 · 8 min read

Ohio doesn't set a cap on late fees for commercial invoices, which means freelancers working under a written contract can charge whatever rate they and their client agreed to — as long as it's reasonable. The absence of a state ceiling is a practical advantage, but it puts more responsibility on you to get the terms right from the start. Here's what Ohio freelancers need to know.

Ohio's Approach to Commercial Late Fees

Unlike some states that specify maximum late fee rates in their statutes, Ohio leaves commercial payment terms largely to the parties involved. For business-to-business transactions — which covers most freelance work — there's no statutory rate ceiling on late charges. Courts apply a general reasonableness standard: a fee that's disproportionate to the original invoice amount or that functions as a penalty rather than a compensation for delay may not be fully enforced.

In practice, Ohio courts and legal practitioners treat 1.5% per month (18% annually) as a widely accepted standard for commercial late fees. Rates up to 2% per month are generally enforceable without issue. If you charge above that threshold, you should be prepared to demonstrate that the rate was clearly disclosed and mutually agreed upon in writing.

Ohio's usury law context: Ohio's usury statutes (ORC Chapter 1343) cap interest on consumer loans, but these limits do not apply to business-to-business commercial transactions. Freelance work is treated as a commercial arrangement, so consumer interest caps are not relevant to your late fee structure.

Ohio's Prompt Payment Act for Government Clients

If you do work for Ohio state agencies or local government bodies, you're covered by the Ohio Prompt Payment Act (ORC 126.30). Under this law, state agencies must pay valid invoices within 30 days of receipt. If payment is late, the agency owes interest at a rate set annually by the Ohio Department of Taxation — typically around 3% to 5% per year depending on the current rate schedule.

This statutory interest applies automatically — you don't need a late fee clause in your contract to be entitled to it. If you're invoicing a state or county agency in Ohio and payment is dragging past 30 days, you're already earning interest on that balance by law.

Private clients are not covered by the Prompt Payment Act. Your rights with a private client depend entirely on your contract.

Does Ohio Have a Freelancer Protection Law?

As of mid-2026, Ohio does not have a statewide freelancer protection law comparable to New York's Freelance Isn't Free Act or Illinois's Freelance Worker Protection Act. Columbus, Cleveland, Cincinnati, and other Ohio cities have not passed equivalent city-level ordinances either.

What this means in practice: In states with freelancer protection laws, violations can result in double damages and attorney fee awards — which creates strong deterrence against non-payment. In Ohio, you're limited to the actual amount owed plus your contractual late fees. This makes a solid written contract your primary enforcement tool.

Ohio is still a contract-friendly state. Well-documented commercial disputes typically resolve in the freelancer's favor in small claims court, particularly when there's a clear written agreement. The lack of a statutory backstop just means you need to do more of the protection work yourself — through your contract terms.

How to Set Up Enforceable Late Fees in Ohio

The requirements for an enforceable late fee in Ohio are straightforward, but getting each element right matters:

  • Written agreement. Your late fee must be specified in writing before work begins — either in your contract or in your standard terms referenced by the contract. An oral agreement about late fees is very difficult to enforce.
  • Specific rate and trigger. State exactly what rate applies and when it starts accruing. "Late fees may apply" is not sufficient. "A late fee of 1.5% per month will accrue on any invoice unpaid after 30 days from the invoice date" is enforceable.
  • Invoice reinforcement. Repeat the late fee terms on every invoice. Accounts payable departments process payments independently — if the terms aren't on the invoice, they may not be aware of them.
  • Written notice when fees begin. Send an email confirming that the invoice is overdue and that late fees are now accruing. This creates a paper trail and often prompts payment without escalation.
  • Updated invoice with fees added. Issue a revised invoice showing the original amount, the late fee calculation, and the updated total. Keep the format professional — late fee invoices that look like debt collection notices can damage the client relationship unnecessarily.

Example late fee clause language

Here's language you can adapt for your contracts:

"Invoices are due within [14/30] days of the invoice date. Any invoice remaining unpaid after the due date will accrue a late fee of 1.5% per month (18% annually) on the outstanding balance. Late fees begin accruing on the first day after the due date and continue until the full balance is received."

Collecting Late Payments in Ohio

Ohio Small Claims Court handles disputes up to $6,000 in Municipal Court. For amounts between $6,000 and $15,000, Municipal Court handles cases with a simplified procedure. Amounts above $15,000 go to Common Pleas Court, where you'll likely want an attorney.

For small claims, you don't need a lawyer, and filing fees are modest. Bring your contract, all invoices, every written communication about the overdue balance, and documentation of when work was completed and delivered. Ohio small claims judges are familiar with business payment disputes, and a well-documented case with a clear written contract typically resolves in the freelancer's favor.

What to bring to small claims court

  • Signed contract (or email chain documenting agreement)
  • Original invoice with your late fee terms visible
  • Updated invoice showing late fees accrued
  • Documentation of work completed and delivered
  • All written communications about the overdue payment
  • Any partial payments received (to show the remaining balance)

Court tiers in Ohio

  • Small Claims: Up to $6,000 — your county Municipal Court, no attorney needed
  • Municipal Court: $6,000–$15,000 — simplified civil procedure
  • Common Pleas: Over $15,000 — consider an attorney

Practical Tips for Ohio Freelancers

  • Include a specific late fee clause in every contract — Ohio has no statutory backstop for private clients, so your contract is doing all the work
  • 1.5% per month is the safe, standard rate; going higher is technically allowed but requires clear mutual agreement in writing
  • Government clients are covered by the Ohio Prompt Payment Act — you're entitled to interest automatically if they pay late, with no fee clause needed
  • Follow up overdue invoices in writing within a week of the due date; early written follow-up significantly improves collection rates and creates the paper trail you need
  • Ohio Small Claims Court (up to $6,000) is accessible and doesn't require a lawyer — don't hesitate to use it for clear-cut non-payment cases
  • Document everything: completed work, delivery confirmation, all client communications about payment
  • Consider pairing your invoice with a written contract that makes your payment terms unambiguous from day one

Ohio's lack of a dedicated freelancer protection law means your contract is doing a lot of the heavy lifting. The good news is that Ohio courts are generally business-friendly and will enforce well-written commercial contracts. Put your terms in writing, be consistent about stating them on every invoice, and follow up promptly when payment is late — that combination handles most situations before they escalate.
