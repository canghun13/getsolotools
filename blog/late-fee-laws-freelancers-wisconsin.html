Wisconsin's Legal Interest Rate

Under Wisconsin Statute § 138.04, the legal rate of interest on money owed is 5% per year when the parties haven't agreed to a different rate in writing. This is the fallback rate a Wisconsin court will apply if your contract is silent on late fees — and 5% annually (well under 0.5% per month) is a weak deterrent for a client who's slow to pay.

The same statute allows parties to contract for a higher rate, so long as it's clearly expressed in writing. Wisconsin's Department of Financial Institutions has also noted that there are no interest rate limitations for credit transactions in Wisconsin when both parties agree to the terms. In practice, freelancers commonly use 1.5% per month (18% annually) as their contractual late fee rate, and it holds up as long as it's disclosed on your contract and invoices. Key distinction: Wisconsin's late-payment-charge rules under § 138.05(3) — which cap certain charges at 1% per month — apply specifically to consumer purchases for individual, family, or household purposes. Freelance service contracts between businesses fall outside that consumer-focused rule, giving you more flexibility to set your own rate by written agreement.

Milwaukee and Wisconsin: No Dedicated Freelancer Protection Law

Wisconsin has no statewide freelancer protection statute equivalent to New York's Freelance Isn't Free Act. Milwaukee, despite a growing base of independent contractors and creative freelancers, has not passed a city ordinance offering dedicated payment protections.

This means Wisconsin freelancers are relying on standard contract enforcement for unpaid invoices. There's no statutory right to double damages, no dedicated city agency for freelance payment disputes, and no simplified administrative complaint process. Your written contract carries the full weight of your protection.

Practical impact: Without a freelancer protection law, deposits and milestone payments matter more in Wisconsin. They reduce how much is ever at risk if a client disputes a final invoice or simply goes quiet.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in Wisconsin

  • Specify your rate in writing. Wisconsin's 5% annual default is low — state your actual rate clearly: "Invoices unpaid after [X] days accrue a late fee of 1.5% per month on the outstanding balance."
  • Choose a grace period upfront. Many freelancers use net-30 terms with a short grace window before fees start accruing. Put whatever you choose directly in the contract.
  • Restate the terms on every invoice. List the due date and late fee rate on the invoice itself — not only in the signed contract — since whoever processes payment may only see the invoice.
  • Send written notice once fees start. A short email confirming the overdue amount and accruing fees creates a clean record and often prompts faster payment.
  • Reissue the invoice with fees added. When following up, send an updated invoice showing the original balance, accrued late fees, and new total due.

Collecting in Wisconsin

Wisconsin Circuit Court handles small claims (governed by Wis. Stat. Chapter 799) for money judgments up to $10,000 — the cap drops to $5,000 for tort and third-party claims. Filing costs are low: a $22 base filing fee plus roughly $5–$15 per defendant for clerk-mailed service, so total upfront cost is typically under $40.

The statute of limitations for written contracts in Wisconsin is 6 years, giving you a reasonable window to pursue an unpaid invoice. Wisconsin's e-filing system is available statewide, and self-representation is standard — most freelancers don't need an attorney for a small claims case.

  • Wisconsin Circuit Court small claims: up to $10,000 for money judgments — no lawyer required
  • Tort/third-party claims: capped at $5,000 separately
  • Filing fee: roughly $22 base, plus service costs
  • Written contract statute of limitations: 6 years

Practical Tips for Wisconsin Freelancers

  • Always specify your late fee rate in writing — Wisconsin's 5% annual default is far lower than what most freelancers charge in practice
  • 1.5% per month (18% annually) is standard and enforceable in Wisconsin under a written agreement
  • Wisconsin has no freelancer protection law — your contract is your main line of defense
  • Consider milestone payments to limit risk; use our Milestone Payment Calculator to plan the split
  • Wisconsin's $22 small claims filing fee is among the lowest in the country, making court an accessible option
  • Follow up on overdue invoices promptly — the 6-year statute of limitations gives you time, but earlier follow-up almost always works better

Wisconsin's default interest rate won't do much on its own, and there's no freelancer protection statute to lean on — but the state doesn't restrict what you can agree to contractually either. A clear late fee clause, consistently restated on your invoices, is enforceable and gives you real leverage when a client pays late.
