Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in Alabama

July 20, 2026 · 7 min read

Alabama is one of the few states where the legal interest rate literally depends on whether you bothered to put anything in writing — 6% if you didn't, 8% if you did. Here's what Alabama freelancers need to know about late fees, from that written-vs-verbal split to the state's $6,000 small claims ceiling.

Alabama's Written-vs-Verbal Interest Rate Split

Ala. Code § 8-8-1 sets Alabama's legal interest rate at 6% per year when there's no written agreement, and 8% per year as the ceiling when the parties do put something in writing without stating a specific rate. Both numbers sit well below the 1.5% monthly (18% annually) rate most freelancers actually charge — which is exactly why the written contract matters here more than in most states. Alabama's usury framework was built primarily around personal loans rather than invoice late fees, and freelance service agreements are generally treated as ordinary contract terms rather than loans, so a clearly disclosed 1.5%/month clause in your contract and invoice is standard practice and holds up without triggering Alabama's harsher usury penalties, which are reserved for lending arrangements that exceed the statutory caps.

Alabama's Small Claims Ceiling Is Modest for a Big State

Alabama's small claims docket caps out at $6,000 (Ala. Code § 12-12-31) — lower than several of its neighbors and enough to catch freelancers who bill larger retainers or multi-month projects off guard. Claims between $6,000 and $20,000 move to the regular District Court civil docket instead, which still doesn't require a lawyer but adds more procedural steps. Filing fees scale with the claim size: as little as $35 for claims at or under $1,500, rising to around $198 for claims in the $3,001–$20,000 range.

On interest rates, Alabama's default figures (6–8%) are actually competitive with Georgia's 7% and lower than Florida's 8%, so the rate itself isn't the disadvantage — it's the venue for collecting when a client won't pay.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in Alabama

  • Put your rate in writing. "Invoices unpaid after 15 days accrue interest at 1.5% per month" clears both of Alabama's statutory defaults and gives you a rate three times higher than the 6% floor.
  • State the rate on every invoice, not just in the original agreement, so it's visible when payment becomes due.
  • Watch the $6,000 line. If a project or retainer is likely to exceed that amount, budget for the regular District Court docket rather than assuming small claims will cover it.
  • File within 6 years. Alabama's statute of limitations for written contracts gives you a solid window to act (Ala. Code § 6-2-34).

Post-judgment interest is a flat 7.5%, regardless of your contract rate — usually. Under Ala. Code § 8-8-10, most money judgments accrue interest at 7.5% per year. The exception: judgments arising from a contract action bear interest at the rate stated in that contract instead, so a written 1.5%/month clause carries through even after you win in court, rather than dropping back to the statutory default.

Collecting in Alabama

Alabama's small claims docket handles disputes up to $6,000 (Ala. Code § 12-12-31), filed in the county where the defendant resides or does business. Larger claims, up to $20,000, go to the regular District Court civil docket.

  • Alabama small claims docket: up to $6,000, filing fee as low as $35 for claims under $1,500
  • Alabama District Court (regular civil docket): for claims between $6,000 and $20,000
  • Written contract statute of limitations: 6 years (Ala. Code § 6-2-34); 10 years for contracts under seal (§ 6-2-33)
  • Post-judgment interest: 7.5% annually, or the contract's own rate if the claim arose from a contract (Ala. Code § 8-8-10)
