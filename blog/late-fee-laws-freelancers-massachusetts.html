Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in Massachusetts

July 16, 2026 · 7 min read

Massachusetts has one of the highest statutory contract interest rates in the country at 12% annually, and gives freelancers a full six years to sue over an unpaid invoice — among the longest windows in the U.S. There's no dedicated freelancer protection act yet, but the state's consumer protection law includes a business-to-business provision that's worth knowing about. Here's what Massachusetts freelancers need to know about charging and collecting late fees.

Massachusetts' 12% Contract Interest Rate

Under Massachusetts General Laws c. 231, § 6C, a court awards 12% annual interest on unpaid contract obligations — calculated from the date of breach or demand, or from when the lawsuit was filed if no earlier date is established. The same 12% rate applies to post-judgment interest under M.G.L. c. 235, § 8. This is significantly higher than the 6-8% default rate most states use, and it applies whether or not your contract mentions interest at all.

That said, 12% is the rate a court applies if you have to sue. It doesn't set what you can charge in your own late fee clause. Most Massachusetts freelancers still write 1.5% per month (18% annually) into their contracts — a rate well within Massachusetts' 20% criminal usury ceiling and standard enough that clients rarely push back.

Six Years to Collect

Massachusetts gives you six years from the date of breach to sue on a written or oral contract (M.G.L. c. 260, § 2) — longer than most states, where the window is often 3 to 4 years. Practically, this means an old unpaid invoice you'd written off elsewhere may still be collectible in Massachusetts. It's not a reason to delay collection, but it's useful to know if a client relationship went sideways months ago and you're only now deciding to pursue it.

Chapter 93A: Massachusetts' Unfair Business Practices Law

Massachusetts' Consumer Protection Act, M.G.L. c. 93A, is best known for protecting consumers, but § 11 of the same chapter extends similar protection to businesses in disputes with other businesses. If a client's non-payment crosses the line from "slow" into a genuinely unfair or deceptive business practice — for example, deliberately stringing you along with false promises to pay, or refusing to pay for delivered work without a good-faith reason — a Chapter 93A claim can allow for double or triple damages plus attorney's fees.

Good to know, not a first move: Chapter 93A claims require a 30-day written demand letter before filing suit (M.G.L. c. 93A, § 9) and are a heavier tool than most freelance payment disputes need. For a straightforward late payment, your late fee clause and small claims court are usually faster and simpler. Save Chapter 93A for situations that look like bad-faith business conduct, not just a slow-paying client.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in Massachusetts

Massachusetts courts enforce late fee clauses the same way most states do — as long as the terms are clear, agreed to before work begins, and not punitive on their face:

  • Put the rate in writing. "Invoices unpaid after 30 days accrue interest at 1.5% per month on the outstanding balance" is specific enough to enforce.
  • Repeat it on every invoice. Clients' accounts payable teams often work from the invoice alone, not the underlying contract.
  • Stay reasonable. 1.5–2% per month is standard commercial practice and well under Massachusetts' 20% criminal usury threshold.
  • Send a written notice before escalating. A polite reminder that references your contract terms protects you if the dispute ever reaches small claims court.

Collecting in Massachusetts

Massachusetts Small Claims Session, part of the District Court, handles disputes up to $7,000 (M.G.L. c. 218, § 21) — with no cap for property damage from a motor vehicle. It's designed for self-represented filers: no attorney required, filing fees range from $40 to $150 depending on the amount claimed, and the clerk-magistrate handles service of the complaint for you.

  • Massachusetts Small Claims Session: up to $7,000 — no attorney required, low filing fee
  • Massachusetts District Court civil docket: for claims above $7,000
  • Chapter 93A demand letter: for cases involving bad-faith or deceptive non-payment, not routine late payment
