Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in Tennessee

Tennessee's Default Rate: 10% — But Written Contracts Can Set Their Own

Under Tennessee's maximum-rate statute (Tenn. Code § 47-14-103), a written contract that references the "formula rate" — a variable rate tied to the average prime lending rate, published monthly by the state — can use that method as its ceiling. In practice, almost no freelance contract does this. Instead, most Tennessee freelancers simply write a flat late fee rate directly into their contract and invoices, such as 1.5% per month (18% annually), which courts enforce as the agreed rate as long as it isn't unconscionable. If a contract is silent on interest entirely, Tennessee defaults to 10% per year (§ 47-14-103(3)) — already higher than the flat default in several neighboring states.

The small claims gap is the more practical difference for freelancers. If you work with clients across the Tennessee–Virginia or Tennessee–North Carolina borders, it's worth knowing which side of the line a dispute might be filed on, since venue is typically determined by where the defendant is located or does business.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in Tennessee

  • Put your rate in writing. "Invoices unpaid after 15 days accrue interest at 1.5% per month" is enforceable and clearer than relying on the 10% statutory default.
  • Reference the rate on every invoice, not just the original contract, so it's visible when the client is deciding whether to pay on time.
  • Keep it reasonable. 1.5–2% per month is standard nationally and well within what Tennessee courts expect from a genuine estimate of damages rather than a punitive penalty.
  • Track invoice dates carefully. Written contract claims must be filed within 6 years under Tenn. Code § 28-3-109(a)(3), so keep dated records for as long as a balance remains open.

Tennessee's $25,000 small claims ceiling is unusually high. Most states cap small claims between $5,000 and $12,000. If a Tennessee client owes you a large balance, don't assume you need a lawyer — General Sessions Court may still be the right venue, saving significant time and legal fees.

Collecting in Tennessee

Tennessee's General Sessions Court — the state's version of small claims court — handles civil money disputes up to $25,000 (Tenn. Code § 16-15-501), with no attorney required, though attorneys are permitted on either side. Four counties (Anderson, Davidson, Hamilton, and Knox) cap General Sessions claims at $15,000 instead, so check your county before filing.

  • Tennessee General Sessions Court: up to $25,000 in most counties ($15,000 in Anderson, Davidson, Hamilton, and Knox) — attorneys allowed but not required
  • Written contract statute of limitations: 6 years (Tenn. Code § 28-3-109(a)(3))
  • Post-judgment interest: accrues at the published formula rate under Tenn. Code § 47-14-121
