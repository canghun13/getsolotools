Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in Arizona

July 16, 2026 · Updated Jul 18, 2026 · 7 min read

Arizona is one of the more permissive states for freelancers setting their own late fee rate — any rate you and your client agree to in writing is enforceable, with no statutory ceiling. The catch is on the collection side: Arizona's small claims court caps out at just $3,500, low enough that many freelance invoices won't qualify. Here's what Arizona freelancers need to know about late fees and getting paid.

Arizona's Interest Rate: 10% Default, No Cap With a Written Agreement

Under A.R.S. § 44-1201, Arizona's default legal rate — the rate that applies to a debt or judgment when no rate was agreed to — is 10% per year. But the same statute lets parties agree to any rate of interest in writing, with no usury ceiling for a properly documented commercial agreement. That makes Arizona one of the more freelancer-friendly states for setting your own terms: as long as your rate is written into your contract before work begins, there's no state-imposed cap to worry about.

Most Arizona freelancers still stick with the nationwide standard of 1.5% per month (18% annually) — well within what's enforceable, and familiar enough that clients rarely push back. A $2,500 invoice overdue by 30 days at 1.5% monthly comes to about $37.50 in late fees; run your own numbers with the calculator above or the full late fee calculator.

The Small Claims Ceiling Is Low — Know Where to File

Arizona's small claims limit is $3,500 — one of the lowest in the country, and a real constraint if your unpaid invoice is larger. If your claim exceeds that, it doesn't mean you're out of options: Arizona's Justice Court handles civil claims up to $10,000 with a similarly informal, accessible process (attorneys are optional, though allowed, unlike small claims division where they're typically barred). Above $10,000, Superior Court handles the case, and hiring an attorney becomes more worthwhile.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in Arizona

  • Put your exact rate in writing before work starts. Because Arizona allows any contractually agreed rate, there's no ambiguity to exploit — but it also means an unwritten rate defaults to just 10%, well below what most freelancers want.
  • Repeat the terms on every invoice so the client's accounts payable team sees them without needing the original contract.
  • Keep the rate defensible. Even without a statutory cap, Arizona courts can decline to enforce a rate that looks punitive rather than compensatory — 1.5–2% per month is safely within normal commercial practice.
  • Know your forum before you file. Check whether your claim fits within the $3,500 small claims cap or needs to go to Justice Court instead.

Collecting in Arizona

  • Arizona Small Claims Court: up to $3,500 — informal, attorneys generally not used
  • Arizona Justice Court: up to $10,000 — still accessible without a lawyer, attorneys allowed
  • Arizona Superior Court: above $10,000 — attorney recommended
  • Default judgment interest: 10% annually, or the contract rate if one was agreed in writing (A.R.S. § 44-1201)

What late fee rate is enforceable in Arizona? +

Arizona has no statutory cap on the interest rate you can charge if it's agreed to in writing (A.R.S. § 44-1201) — the 10% annual rate only applies by default when no rate was specified. Most freelancers charge 1.5% per month (18% annually), which is standard and safely enforceable.

Where do I file if my unpaid invoice is over $3,500 in Arizona? +

Arizona's small claims court caps out at $3,500. For claims between roughly $3,500 and $10,000, file in Justice Court instead — it's still designed to be accessible without a lawyer, though attorneys are permitted there.

How does Arizona's small claims limit compare to nearby states? +

Arizona's $3,500 small claims limit is strikingly low next to its neighbors — California allows up to $12,500 for individuals and Nevada allows up to $10,000. An Arizona freelancer with an invoice much above $3,500 will likely need Justice Court (up to $10,000) instead of true small claims.
