Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in North Carolina

July 16, 2026 · 7 min read

North Carolina sets its default interest rate at 8% annually and gives freelancers a $10,000 small claims court — one of the more generous limits in the country. The tradeoff is a short statute of limitations: written contract claims must be filed within three years, well below the 4-6 year window many other states allow. Here's what North Carolina freelancers need to know about charging and collecting late fees.

North Carolina's 8% Legal Interest Rate

Under N.C. Gen. Stat. § 24-1 and § 24-5, the legal interest rate is 8% per year — this is both the rate that applies when a contract doesn't specify one, and the default post-judgment rate. Parties are free to agree to a different rate in writing, and most freelancers still use the nationwide standard of 1.5% per month (18% annually), which is enforceable in North Carolina as long as it's clearly stated in your contract and on your invoices before work begins.

A $3,000 invoice that goes 45 days overdue at 1.5% monthly accrues about $67.50 in late fees — use the calculator above (or the full late fee calculator) to run your own numbers.

A Shorter Window to Collect

North Carolina's statute of limitations for written contract claims is three years from the date of breach (N.C. Gen. Stat. § 1-52(1)) — shorter than the 4-6 years common in many other states. If a client has gone dark on an invoice, this is a real reason not to let it sit indefinitely. Send a written demand promptly, keep records of every attempt to collect, and don't wait years to decide whether the amount is worth pursuing.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in North Carolina

North Carolina courts enforce late fee clauses that are specific, written, and agreed to before the work starts:

  • State the exact rate. "Invoices unpaid after 30 days accrue interest at 1.5% per month on the outstanding balance" holds up; a vague "late fees may apply" does not.
  • Put it in the contract, not just the invoice. The clause needs to be agreed to before work begins to be fully enforceable.
  • Keep the rate reasonable. 1.5–2% per month tracks national norms and is well inside what North Carolina courts consider defensible.
  • Repeat the terms on every invoice so accounts payable sees them without digging through the original contract.

Collecting in North Carolina

North Carolina's small claims court — heard by a magistrate as part of District Court — handles disputes up to $10,000 (N.C.G.S. § 7A-210), a higher ceiling than most states, which means many freelance invoice disputes fit comfortably within it. The filing fee is a flat $96 statewide (as of 2024), and no attorney is required. Either side can appeal a magistrate's decision to a full District Court trial (de novo) within 10 days.

  • North Carolina Small Claims (Magistrate Court): up to $10,000 — flat $96 filing fee, no attorney required
  • North Carolina District/Superior Court: for claims above $10,000
  • Post-judgment interest: 8% annually unless your contract specifies a higher rate (N.C.G.S. § 24-1)

What late fee rate is enforceable in North Carolina? +

If your contract doesn't specify a rate, North Carolina defaults to 8% annually (N.C. Gen. Stat. § 24-1, § 24-5). Most freelancers charge 1.5% per month (18% annually) instead, which is enforceable as long as it's clearly stated in your contract and invoice before work begins.

How long do I have to sue over an unpaid invoice in North Carolina? +

Three years from the date of breach for written contract claims (N.C. Gen. Stat. § 1-52(1)) — shorter than many states, so don't let an unpaid invoice sit for too long before deciding whether to pursue it.
