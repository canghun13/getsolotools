Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in Colorado

June 1, 2026 · 6 min read

Colorado has one of the more freelancer-friendly legal interest rate defaults in the country at 8% annually, and its Prompt Payment Act covers a broad range of government contracts. Denver has also been active on gig worker policy in recent years. Here's what Colorado freelancers need to know about charging and collecting late fees.

Colorado's Legal Interest Rate

Colorado's statutory interest rate (C.R.S. § 5-12-102) is 8% per year when no rate is specified in a contract. That's about 0.67% per month — notably higher than states like Michigan (5%) or Pennsylvania (6%), but still below the commercial standard that most freelancers charge.

For commercial transactions between businesses, Colorado allows parties to agree to a higher rate in writing. The standard 1.5% per month (18% annually) is enforceable in Colorado with a clear written agreement. A $4,000 invoice unpaid for 45 days at 1.5% monthly accrues $90 in late fees. Colorado's higher default works in your favor: Even if you forget to specify a rate in a particular contract, Colorado's 8% annual default is meaningful enough to matter on larger invoices. That said, always specify your rate — 8% annually is still far below what you'd charge with a proper late fee clause.

Colorado's Prompt Payment Act for Government Clients

Colorado's Prompt Payment Act (C.R.S. § 24-91-103) is one of the more comprehensive in the country. State agencies must pay undisputed invoices within 45 days of receipt. If payment is late, the state owes interest at the rate specified in the contract, or at 8% annually if no rate is specified.

The Act also covers construction contracts and extends to subcontractors and sub-subcontractors on government projects. If you do any work on a Colorado government project as a subcontractor, you're entitled to payment within 7 days of the prime contractor receiving payment — and to interest on late payment if that timeline isn't met.

For local government clients (city contracts, county agencies), similar prompt payment requirements apply under Colorado procurement rules, though the specific deadlines vary by jurisdiction. Always confirm payment terms before starting work with any government client in Colorado.

Denver Gig Worker Protections

Denver passed a gig worker protection ordinance (Denver Revised Municipal Code Chapter 28) that primarily targets app-based gig platforms — rideshare, delivery, and similar services. It establishes minimum pay standards and transparency requirements for those platforms.

This ordinance does not directly apply to most freelancers in the traditional sense — it's focused on platform-based gig work, not independent contractors working with direct business clients. However, it reflects a broader legislative trend in Colorado toward stronger worker protections, and Denver has shown willingness to act on gig economy issues that the state has not yet addressed statewide.

What this means for freelancers: Colorado's legislative environment is becoming more worker-friendly. While there's no statewide freelancer protection act yet, the trajectory suggests additional protections may emerge. For now, Colorado freelancers working with private clients rely on contract law and civil court — the same framework as most states without a dedicated freelancer statute.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in Colorado

Colorado courts enforce commercial contracts as written. For a late fee clause to hold:

  • Written and specific. State your rate, the trigger date, and how fees compound. "A late fee of 1.5% per month accrues on balances unpaid after 30 days from the invoice date" is enforceable. Vague references to late fees are not.
  • Pre-work agreement. The clause must be in your contract signed before work begins, not added unilaterally on an invoice after the fact.
  • Reasonable rate. Colorado courts apply a reasonableness standard. 1.5% to 2% per month is well within accepted commercial practice. Rates significantly above 2% per month could face scrutiny.
  • Invoice reinforcement. Repeat your payment terms and late fee policy on every invoice. Accounts payable departments often work from invoices alone, not from the underlying contract.
  • Written follow-up. When a payment goes overdue, follow up in writing promptly. Confirm the overdue amount, the date fees began accruing, and the updated total owed.

Collecting in Colorado

Colorado Small Claims Court handles disputes up to $7,500. You don't need an attorney, and the process is designed to be accessible for individuals and small businesses. Filing fees are low, and cases are typically scheduled within 30 to 60 days.

For amounts between $7,500 and $25,000, the County Court handles civil cases with a simplified procedure. Above $25,000, District Court handles larger civil disputes. For amounts above the small claims threshold, consulting an attorney before filing is worthwhile.

  • Colorado Small Claims Court: up to $7,500 — no attorney required
  • Colorado County Court: $7,500–$25,000
  • Colorado District Court: over $25,000 — consider an attorney
  • Colorado Attorney General: for fraud patterns, not individual payment disputes

Practical Tips for Colorado Freelancers

  • Colorado's default rate of 8% annually is higher than most states — but still always specify a higher commercial rate in your contract
  • 1.5% per month is the standard commercial rate and is enforceable in Colorado with a written agreement
  • Government clients fall under Colorado's Prompt Payment Act — 45-day payment requirement with statutory interest on late payment
  • Denver's gig worker ordinance covers platform-based gig workers, not traditional freelancers — but Colorado's legislative trend is toward stronger worker protections
  • Small Claims Court (up to $7,500) is accessible without a lawyer
  • Document deliverables and client approvals in writing — email approvals are your strongest evidence in any dispute

Colorado's relatively high statutory interest rate and strong Prompt Payment Act make it a reasonably freelancer-friendly state from a legal standpoint. The missing piece — a statewide freelancer protection act — means private-client disputes still rely on contract enforcement and civil court. Keep your contracts clear, follow up promptly in writing when payment is late, and don't hesitate to use Small Claims Court when a client crosses the line from slow to non-paying.
