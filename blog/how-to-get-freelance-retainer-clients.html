How to Get Freelance Retainer Clients (And Keep Them)

Jun 29, 2026 · 10 min read

Project-based freelancing is exhausting in a specific way. You finish a project, get paid, and then immediately need to find the next one. The income is real but the stability isn't — and over time, the constant cycle of pitching, closing, delivering, and starting over grinds you down.

Retainer clients solve this. Instead of billing for a project, you bill for ongoing availability — a fixed monthly fee for a defined scope of work or set number of hours. Done right, a handful of retainer clients can give you more financial stability than a full calendar of one-off projects, with significantly less time spent on sales.

Here's how to get them, pitch them, structure the agreement, and keep them long-term.

What a Freelance Retainer Actually Is

A retainer is a monthly agreement where a client pays a fixed fee in exchange for a defined scope of ongoing work. The most common structures are:

  • Deliverable-based: A set number of outputs per month — four blog posts, two design deliverables, 10 hours of development. The clearest structure and easiest to track.
  • Hours-based: A block of your time each month, billed regardless of what specific projects come up. More flexible for clients, but creates the "why am I paying for hours I didn't use?" problem.
  • Outcome-based: Less common, but for some freelancers (especially consultants), billing for a monthly outcome rather than hours or deliverables is the most defensible model.

Deliverable-based retainers are generally easier to sell and manage. Clients understand exactly what they're getting. You know exactly what you're committing to. There's no ambiguity about whether time is being "used" each month.

The key difference between a retainer and a project: In a project, you deliver something specific and the relationship ends. In a retainer, you deliver something ongoing and the relationship continues until one party ends it. That continuity is what makes retainers valuable — for both sides.

Why Clients Say Yes to Retainers

Before pitching a retainer, you need to understand why a client would want one. The reasons are more client-centered than most freelancers realize.

Guaranteed access. When you're on retainer, the client doesn't have to compete for your time or worry that you're booked out. They know you're available each month. For clients who rely on consistent output — content, design, code — this is genuinely valuable.

Budget predictability. A fixed monthly fee is easier to budget than unpredictable project costs. Finance teams and business owners prefer known expenses over variable ones. A retainer turns your services into a line item they can plan around.

Reduced onboarding friction. Every new freelancer relationship requires explaining context, preferences, brand voice, workflows. A retainer client doesn't have to re-explain themselves every month. The longer the relationship, the faster and better the work gets — which is in the client's direct interest.

Priority service. Retainer clients typically get faster turnaround and first access to your calendar. If they need something quickly, they're not waiting in a queue behind one-off project clients.

Which Clients to Target

Not every client is a good retainer candidate. The best retainer clients share a few characteristics:

  • They have recurring needs — they need your type of work every month, not just once
  • They pay reliably — late payments on a one-off project are annoying; on a retainer, they're a serious problem
  • They communicate well — a retainer relationship is long-term; poor communication compounds over months
  • They respect your work — clients who undervalue you on a project will undervalue you even more as familiarity grows
  • They have budget stability — a startup in its first month isn't an ideal retainer client; an established business with recurring marketing needs is

Look at your existing and past clients first. The best retainer clients are almost always people you've already worked with successfully. You've proven yourself, they know your work, and the trust is established. Pitching a retainer to a first-time client is harder and riskier for both sides.

How to Pitch a Retainer

Most freelancers make the mistake of pitching a retainer too early — after one project, before trust is built. The right time is after you've completed at least one or two successful projects with a client and there's an obvious ongoing need.

Step 1: Identify the ongoing need

Before pitching anything, ask yourself: what does this client need every month? If you're a content writer, maybe they need four blog posts per month. If you're a designer, maybe they need social media assets updated weekly. If you're a developer, maybe they need ongoing maintenance and new features.

The retainer offer should map directly to that recurring need — not to what you want to sell, but to what they demonstrably need on an ongoing basis.

Step 2: Frame it around their benefit

The pitch should focus on what the client gains, not on what you get. Lead with guaranteed availability, budget predictability, and the efficiency that comes from a long-term relationship. Don't lead with "this gives me stable income" — even if that's true.

A simple pitch might look like this:

"I've really enjoyed working on [project] with you. I noticed that you have an ongoing need for [type of work] each month. I'd love to offer you a monthly retainer — [specific deliverables] for $[amount]/month. This guarantees you consistent output, priority access to my calendar, and a slight discount versus booking individual projects. Would you be open to discussing it?"

Step 3: Make the terms crystal clear

Vague retainer agreements create conflict. Before a client signs, both parties need to agree explicitly on:

  • What deliverables or hours are included each month
  • What happens to unused hours or deliverables (roll over? expire? partial credit?)
  • How additional work beyond scope is priced
  • When payment is due each month
  • How much notice is required to cancel
  • What happens if the client goes quiet or doesn't provide needed inputs

Put all of this in a written agreement — not just an email confirmation. A clear retainer contract protects both sides and prevents the most common retainer disputes.

Structuring the Retainer Agreement

A solid retainer agreement doesn't need to be long. The key clauses to include are:

Scope of work: Exactly what's included each month. Be specific. "Ongoing design support" is not a scope. "Up to 8 hours of design work per month, including up to 4 rounds of revisions on agreed deliverables" is a scope.

Rollover policy: What happens if the client doesn't use all their hours or deliverables? The cleanest approach is no rollover — each month is paid for and used within that month. Rollover creates tracking complexity and can lead to clients "banking" work and then requesting it all at once.

Overage rate: What you charge for work that exceeds the monthly scope. This should be your standard rate, not your retainer rate. Clients who regularly exceed scope should either upgrade their retainer or pay standard rates for the extra work.

Notice period: How much advance notice is required to cancel. 30 days is standard for most freelance retainers. For complex or high-value retainers, 60 days is reasonable.

Payment timing: Most retainers are paid at the start of the month, in advance. This protects you from clients who cancel mid-month after consuming your time. Payment on the first of the month is clean and easy to budget on both sides.

Pricing Your Retainer

Retainer pricing should be based on your standard hourly or project rate, not a significant discount. Many freelancers make the mistake of deeply discounting retainer work in exchange for "guaranteed income" — but the guarantee isn't worth 30-40% off your rate.

A reasonable retainer discount is 10-15% off your standard rate. This reflects the value of reliable, recurring work without dramatically reducing your effective hourly rate. If you're offering more than 15% off, reconsider — the stability of the retainer is valuable to you, but not so valuable that you should work significantly below your normal rate indefinitely.

For a deliverable-based retainer, price it as you would price the deliverables individually, apply a modest discount, and that's your monthly fee. Keep it simple.

The Trial Period

For new retainer relationships (even with existing clients), consider offering a 60- or 90-day trial period before locking into a longer-term agreement. This lets both parties test the arrangement before committing.

A trial period is particularly useful because retainer relationships feel different than project relationships. The pace changes, the communication rhythm changes, and sometimes what seemed like a perfect retainer fit turns out not to work as well as expected. A trial exit clause removes that risk for both sides and actually makes clients more willing to say yes.

How to Keep Retainer Clients Long-Term

Getting the retainer is the beginning, not the end. The most common reason retainers end is that the client stops seeing clear value — the output becomes routine, the relationship becomes transactional, or the client's needs shift and the retainer scope no longer fits.

Send a monthly summary. Even for simple retainers, a brief monthly summary of what was delivered and any results or progress builds perceived value. It makes the work visible rather than invisible.

Flag scope changes early. If the client's needs are expanding or shifting, surface it proactively. Propose a scope adjustment before things drift. Clients who feel like a retainer is no longer right-sized will quietly start looking for an exit.

Raise rates annually. Don't let a retainer lock you into last year's rate indefinitely. Build a rate review into your annual calendar and give retainer clients the same advance notice you'd give any client.

Stay proactive. The best retainer freelancers don't just wait for the client to tell them what to do. They bring ideas, flag problems before they escalate, and act more like a partner than a vendor. That's what makes retainer relationships sticky.

Watch for scope creep. The most common problem in retainer relationships is gradual scope expansion. Small requests accumulate until you're doing significantly more work than the retainer covers. Track your time and output monthly. When you consistently exceed scope, have the conversation about adjusting the retainer — don't absorb the extra work indefinitely.

What to Do When a Retainer Isn't Working

Sometimes retainers don't work out. The client's business changes, the need disappears, or the relationship dynamic doesn't translate well to an ongoing arrangement. That's okay.

If a retainer isn't working, end it professionally and on good terms. Give proper notice, complete any outstanding work, and leave the client with everything they need to continue without you. A retainer that ends well is still a relationship asset — former retainer clients often refer new clients or return when their needs change.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much should I charge for a freelance retainer?

Base your retainer on your standard rate for the included deliverables or hours, then apply a 10-15% discount in exchange for the reliability of recurring work. Don't discount more than that — the stability is valuable to you, but not so valuable that you should work significantly below market rate indefinitely.

When is the right time to pitch a retainer?

After completing at least one or two successful projects with a client where you can clearly see an ongoing need. Don't pitch after the first project — you haven't yet established enough trust or demonstrated enough value for the client to commit to a monthly agreement.

What if a client doesn't use all their retainer hours?

In a well-structured retainer, unused hours or deliverables don't roll over — they expire at the end of the month. This should be stated clearly in your contract. If a client consistently doesn't use their full retainer, it's worth discussing a scope reduction rather than having them pay for capacity they're not using.

How do I handle extra work beyond the retainer scope?

Bill it at your standard rate (not the discounted retainer rate) and get explicit approval before starting. Your retainer contract should state the overage rate clearly so there are no surprises. Track scope carefully — if a client regularly requests more than the retainer covers, propose a scope upgrade.

Can I have multiple retainer clients?

Yes — and most established freelancers aim for two to four retainer clients that together cover their baseline income. The key is making sure the total committed hours don't exceed your realistic capacity, leaving room for one-off projects and non-billable time.

What's the difference between a retainer and a subscription service?

Functionally, they're very similar. A retainer is the traditional term used in professional services (legal, consulting, freelancing). Some freelancers use "subscription" or "monthly package" because it's more immediately understood by clients who aren't familiar with retainer agreements. The underlying structure — fixed monthly fee for defined ongoing work — is the same.
