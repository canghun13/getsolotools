Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in Florida

May 19, 2026 · 6 min read

Florida gives freelancers more flexibility than most states when it comes to late fees — there's no statutory cap on what you can charge for commercial invoices. But that freedom comes with a catch: courts apply a reasonableness standard, and if your rate looks punitive, it may not hold up. Here's what you need to know to charge late fees confidently and legally in Florida.

Does Florida Have a Late Fee Cap for Freelancers?

For commercial transactions — which covers most freelance work — Florida law does not set a maximum late fee. You and your client are free to agree to any rate in writing. This is different from residential leases, where Florida law caps late fees at 5% of the rent due (Fla. Stat. § 83.45).

The practical limit is the reasonableness standard. Florida courts will evaluate late fees as a form of liquidated damages — a pre-agreed estimate of harm caused by late payment. A fee that looks disproportionate to the invoice amount or seems designed to punish rather than compensate can be challenged or reduced by a court.

What Rate Should Florida Freelancers Charge?

The standard freelance late fee rate is 1.5% per month on the outstanding balance, which works out to 18% per year. This rate is widely recognized, easy to explain to clients, and well within what Florida courts would consider reasonable for business-to-business transactions.

Some freelancers prefer a flat fee — typically $25 to $50 per late invoice — especially on smaller projects where a percentage would amount to just a few dollars. Either approach is valid as long as it's clearly stated in writing before work begins.

Florida also has no legally mandated grace period for commercial invoices. However, offering 5–7 days before fees kick in is standard practice and reduces friction with clients who are slow but not intentionally withholding payment.

No Freelance Protection Law — What That Means

Unlike New York, California, and Illinois, Florida does not have a statewide Freelance Worker Protection Act. There's no law that automatically entitles you to double damages or attorney's fees if a client pays late. Your protections come from your contract and general contract law.

This makes it even more important that your agreements are solid from the start. In Florida, your contract is your primary enforcement tool.

Note: If your client is based in a state that does have freelancer protection laws — such as New York or Illinois — those laws may apply to you even if you're located in Florida, depending on where the work was performed or where the client operates. Worth checking if you're dealing with an out-of-state client.

How to Make Late Fees Enforceable in Florida

Florida courts will enforce late fees if they're properly documented. Here's how to set things up correctly:

  • Put it in your contract. Include a clear clause: "Invoices unpaid after [X] days will accrue interest at 1.5% per month on the outstanding balance." Vague language like "late fees may apply" is not enough.
  • Repeat it on every invoice. List payment terms, due date, and late fee rate directly on the invoice. This ensures the client saw the terms at the time of billing.
  • Send a written reminder before applying fees. An email or written notice creates a paper trail and strengthens your position if you ever need to pursue the matter further.
  • Issue an updated invoice when fees kick in. Show the original amount, the accrued late fee, and the new total. Keep this clean and professional.

Collecting Overdue Invoices in Florida

If a client refuses to pay after multiple attempts, Florida Small Claims Court handles disputes up to $8,000. Filing costs are low — typically $30–$100 depending on the amount — and you don't need a lawyer to represent yourself.

For larger amounts, the Civil Court handles cases up to $30,000 (County Court) or above (Circuit Court). At these levels, an attorney may be worth the cost.

  • Bring your contract, all invoices, and every written communication about the debt
  • Show you made reasonable attempts to collect before going to court
  • Document when the work was completed and when payment was due

Florida also allows you to place a lien on a client's property in certain situations — particularly in construction and service industries. If your work involves improvements to real property, look into Florida's lien laws as an additional avenue.

Practical Tips for Florida Freelancers

  • Always use a written contract — verbal agreements are nearly impossible to enforce
  • State your late fee rate in at least two places: the contract and the invoice
  • Send invoices promptly after completing work — delays on your end weaken your position
  • Follow up in writing (email is fine) so you have a timestamped record
  • Don't let invoices go past 90 days without escalating — older debts are harder to collect
  • Be willing to negotiate. 80% of what you're owed is better than a prolonged dispute

Florida's flexibility on late fees is a genuine advantage for freelancers — but only if you've documented everything properly. Set your terms upfront, put them in writing, and you'll have a solid foundation to collect what you're owed.
