Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in New Jersey

May 27, 2026 · 7 min read

New Jersey is one of the stronger states for freelancer payment protections. The New Jersey Freelance Worker Protection Act (FWPA), which took effect in 2023, gives independent contractors statutory rights that most other states don't provide — including mandatory written contracts, payment deadlines, and meaningful remedies when clients don't pay. Here's what NJ freelancers need to know.

The New Jersey Freelance Worker Protection Act

New Jersey's FWPA applies to freelancers who are hired as independent contractors to provide services worth $250 or more, either as a single contract or as multiple contracts totaling $250 or more in a 120-day period. If that threshold is met, the following rights apply:

  • The hiring party must provide a written contract specifying the services to be performed, the value of the services or rate of payment, and the payment due date.
  • Payment must be made by the date specified in the contract, or within 30 days of completing the services if no date is specified.
  • Clients cannot retaliate against freelancers who assert their rights under the FWPA.
  • Freelancers can file a complaint with the New Jersey Department of Labor and Workforce Development, or bring a civil action directly in court.

Who qualifies: The FWPA applies to individuals (not corporations or LLCs) performing freelance work for a client that is not their employer. It covers the broad range of independent contracting work — design, writing, consulting, photography, development, and more. It does not apply if the freelancer has employees of their own.

Remedies Under the NJ FWPA

What makes the New Jersey law meaningful is the remedy structure. If a client violates the FWPA, freelancers are entitled to:

  • Double damages — twice the amount of unpaid compensation
  • Attorney's fees and costs — the client pays your legal costs if you win
  • Injunctive relief — a court order requiring compliance
  • Civil penalties of up to $1,000 for a first violation, $5,000 for subsequent violations (payable to the state)

The double damages provision is a powerful deterrent. A client who owes you $3,000 and ignores your invoices faces a potential liability of $6,000 plus your attorney's fees if you pursue a FWPA claim. Sophisticated clients know this — mentioning the FWPA when following up on overdue payment often accelerates resolution.

Late Fees on Top of the FWPA

The FWPA establishes your right to be paid on time, but it doesn't automatically include late fees on top of that — late fees still need to be in your contract. New Jersey law doesn't cap commercial late fee rates, so you can specify whatever rate you and your client agree to. The standard 1.5% per month (18% annually) is widely used and clearly enforceable.

Structure it this way: the FWPA protects your right to payment by the contractual due date, and your late fee clause compensates you for the additional harm caused by delay. Both operate independently. A client who violates the FWPA can owe you the unpaid amount, doubled, plus any accumulated late fees under your contract.

New Jersey's default interest rate: Under N.J.S.A. 31:1-1, the legal rate of interest in New Jersey is 6% per year when no rate is specified. For commercial transactions, this can be contractually varied upward. If you don't specify a late fee rate in your contract, 6% annually is what a court would typically apply — far below the 18% most freelancers charge. Always specify your rate in writing.

Filing a FWPA Complaint

New Jersey freelancers can file a complaint with the NJ Department of Labor and Workforce Development's Division of Wage and Hour Compliance. The complaint process is administrative — you're not required to hire a lawyer, though you may. Alternatively, you can file a civil action directly in court, which gives you access to the full remedy package including attorney's fees.

Before filing formally, send a written demand referencing the FWPA. Many clients — especially businesses that work with freelancers regularly — will settle quickly when they realize the legal exposure. Keep all written communication professional and document every step.

New Jersey Small Claims Court

New Jersey Special Civil Part (Small Claims) handles disputes up to $3,000 without a lawyer. For amounts between $3,000 and $20,000, the Special Civil Part handles larger claims with a slightly more formal process. Above $20,000, you're in Superior Court.

For most freelance payment disputes, the Special Civil Part is the practical venue. FWPA claims — which can include double damages — are better filed as a civil action or FWPA complaint rather than small claims, since the double damages remedy requires the full civil process.

  • NJ Special Civil Part Small Claims: up to $3,000
  • NJ Special Civil Part: $3,000–$20,000
  • Superior Court: over $20,000
  • NJ Dept. of Labor FWPA complaint: for FWPA violations — no dollar cap

Practical Tips for New Jersey Freelancers

  • Always use a written contract — it's legally required under the FWPA for projects worth $250 or more
  • Include your late fee rate in the contract (the NJ default of 6% annually is too low for most freelancers)
  • Mention the FWPA in your follow-up when invoices go overdue — it signals you know your rights and often accelerates payment
  • The double damages provision is your biggest leverage point — clients facing FWPA exposure often settle quickly
  • File a FWPA complaint or civil action for non-payment; small claims is better for straightforward disputes without FWPA issues
  • Document everything: signed contracts, delivered work, payment deadlines, all client communications

New Jersey's Freelance Worker Protection Act puts NJ freelancers in a stronger position than most states allow. The written contract requirement, 30-day payment deadline, and double damages remedy create a framework that actually has consequences when ignored. Know your rights under the FWPA, put your terms in writing, and don't hesitate to reference the law when clients drag their feet on payment.
