Late Fee Laws for Freelancers in Georgia

May 27, 2026 · 6 min read

Georgia has a more specific legal framework for interest on unpaid commercial debts than many states, which actually works in freelancers' favor. The state's legal interest rate applies as a default when no rate is specified in writing, and parties can agree to higher rates for business transactions. Here's what Georgia freelancers need to know about structuring and enforcing late fees.

Georgia's Legal Interest Rate

Under Georgia law (O.C.G.A. § 7-4-2), the legal rate of interest is 7% per year when no other rate is specified in a contract. For commercial transactions — which includes most freelance work — parties can contractually agree to a higher rate. In practice, this means if you don't specify a late fee rate in your contract, Georgia courts will apply 7% annually as a default on overdue amounts.

Seven percent annually (less than 0.6% per month) is a modest deterrent. Most freelancers charge more than this as a matter of practice, and Georgia law allows it — you just need to put it in writing. The standard commercial rate of 1.5% per month (18% annually) is enforceable in Georgia with a clear contractual agreement. Rates above 18% annually are permissible for commercial transactions but should be disclosed prominently.

Key distinction: Georgia's usury caps apply to consumer loans and certain other consumer financial products. Business-to-business commercial transactions — including freelance service agreements — are exempt from consumer usury limits under Georgia law. This gives freelancers more flexibility to negotiate payment terms.

Georgia's Prompt Payment Act

Georgia has a Prompt Payment Act (O.C.G.A. § 13-11-1 et seq.) that was primarily designed for construction contracts, but its principles apply more broadly to government contracting situations. For state agency clients, Georgia requires payment within a specified period, and late payment triggers statutory interest.

If you're doing work for Georgia state agencies or local government bodies, review the specific payment terms in your contract carefully. State contracts often include standard prompt payment provisions. For private clients, your contract controls everything — Georgia's Prompt Payment Act doesn't extend automatic protections to freelance services outside of construction-related work.

Atlanta and Georgia: No Dedicated Freelancer Protection Law

Georgia currently has no statewide freelancer protection statute equivalent to New York's Freelance Isn't Free Act. Atlanta, despite having a large and growing freelance and creative workforce, has not passed a city ordinance providing dedicated contractor payment protections.

This means Georgia freelancers working with private clients are relying on standard contract enforcement. There are no double damages provisions, no simplified administrative filing process, and no state agency dedicated to freelancer payment disputes. Your written contract is your primary protection.

Practical impact: Without a freelancer protection law, your leverage in a payment dispute comes from your contract and your willingness to use small claims court. This makes upfront deposits and milestone payments especially important for Georgia freelancers — they reduce the amount at risk if a client disputes a final invoice or simply delays payment.

Setting Up Enforceable Late Fees in Georgia

To have an enforceable late fee structure in Georgia:

  • Specify the rate in your contract. If you don't, Georgia defaults to 7% annually — likely lower than you want. State the rate clearly: "Invoices unpaid after [X] days accrue a late fee of 1.5% per month on the outstanding balance."
  • Include a grace period if you want one. Many freelancers offer a net-30 payment term with a 5 or 10-day grace period before fees accrue. Whatever you choose, make it explicit in the contract.
  • Restate terms on every invoice. Print the due date and late fee rate on the invoice itself, not just in the contract. This matters if the invoice is processed by accounts payable personnel who haven't read the contract.
  • Send a written notice when fees begin accruing. An email or formal letter confirming the overdue amount and that late fees are now running gives you a clean paper trail and often prompts payment.
  • Issue an updated invoice. When sending a collections follow-up, attach a new invoice showing the original amount, late fees calculated to date, and the new total due. Keep it professional and factual.

Collecting in Georgia

Georgia Magistrate Court handles small claims up to $15,000, and you don't need a lawyer to file. Filing fees are low (typically under $100), and cases are usually scheduled within 30 to 60 days of filing. For larger amounts, you'll file in Superior Court, where legal representation becomes more important.

Georgia's Magistrate Court is a practical option for freelancers with unpaid invoices. Bring your contract, all invoices, records of completed work (emails, deliverables, client approvals), and documentation of all attempts to collect. Judges in business disputes are experienced with commercial non-payment cases.

  • Georgia Magistrate Court: up to $15,000 — no lawyer required
  • Superior Court: amounts above $15,000 — consider legal representation
  • Georgia Department of Law Consumer Protection Division: for patterns of fraud, not individual payment disputes

Practical Tips for Georgia Freelancers

  • Always specify your late fee rate in writing — the Georgia default of 7% annually is lower than most freelancers want
  • 1.5% per month (18% annually) is the practical standard; it's enforceable in Georgia with clear contract language
  • Georgia has no freelancer protection law — your written contract is your primary enforcement tool
  • Consider milestone payment structures to limit risk; use our Milestone Payment Calculator to plan the split
  • Georgia Magistrate Court (up to $15,000) is accessible and doesn't require an attorney
  • Follow up overdue invoices quickly in writing — delays make collection harder regardless of how strong your legal position is

Georgia's legal framework is freelancer-friendly in some ways — you can set a commercial late fee rate well above the legal default, and Magistrate Court gives you a practical, low-cost enforcement option. The missing piece is a dedicated freelancer protection statute with teeth. Until one exists, strong contracts and consistent follow-up are your best defense.
